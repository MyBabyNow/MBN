Breastfeeding

You might not have thought much about whether you will breastfeed your baby just yet, and that's OK! But, whether you've thought about it or not, your body will be gearing up to breastfeed, so it's a good idea to know what to expect.

Breastfeeding is a learned skill
Because breastfeeding is so natural, you'd be forgiven for thinking that as soon as your baby is born and placed near your breast, it will all just happen and that's that.

But, the reality is that just like riding a bike or playing a musical instrument, breastfeeding is a skill that we must learn in order to do it successfully. In fact, it can take a mum and her baby 6-8 weeks to figure out what works best for them!

It's quite common to find breastfeeding hard but it is also important to persist - the majority of problems can be overcome and there is no need to feel like a failure and give up. There's also no need to worry if your own mum was unable to breastfeed - it's not genetic, so there's no reason you won't be able to.

Many new mums also don't realise that there is plenty of support available (much of it free) to help them breastfeed their baby - which is hardly surprising given that breastmilk is the only food that a new baby needs until they're around 6 months old!

Benefits of breastmilk and breastfeeding

There are many benefits to breastfeeding, and not just for you and your baby!

Breastfeeding can help you and your partner:

  • save money and time - you don't need to buy breastmilk, so it's far cheaper than using formula (a single tin of formula costs around $25 and once open, must be used within a month), and because breastmilk is always clean, warm and ready, it's also far more convenient. As an added bonus, you won't need to sterilise bottles and teats, boil water or mix formula and, there is much less washing up!
  • get more sleep - mums who breastfeed tend to get back to sleep more quickly and easily than mums who bottle feed (and there's no need for someone to spend time warming up the milk!).
  • with changing nappies - your baby's poo should be softer and easier to clean up (and not as smelly).

It can also help you to:

  • bond with your baby - the skin-to-skin contact gives you a physical connection with your baby and enhances bonding and attachment.
  • recover after pregnancy - the hormones produced when you breastfeed help to contract your uterus back down to its pre-pregnant size and shape within a few weeks after you give birth, reducing the chances of excessive bleeding after delivery.
  • lose weight - making breastmilk uses lots of energy (kilojoules/calories) so it helps you to lose excess weight you may have gained during pregnancy.
  • lower your risk of illness later in life - breastfeeding lowers your risk of developing certain cancers (including breast and ovarian), osteoporosis and type 2 diabetes.

Your baby benefits from breastfeeding because:

  • breastmilk is their perfect food - it is easily digested, contains all the fluid, energy and nutrition they need until they're around 6 months old and, as they grow, it continues to change to meet all their nutritional needs.
  • it boosts their immune system - breastmilk contains a special blend of antibodies that helps protect your baby from illnesses such as gastroenteritis, chest infections, ear infections and urine infections. Eczema, asthma, obesity and type 1 diabetes are all less likely in babies who are breastfed.
  • it protects against sudden unexpected death in infancy (SUDI) - the risk of dying from SUDI reduces by more than 50% in babies who are breastfed.
  • it promotes emotional security and brain development - the bonding and attachment of skin-to-skin contact with you is comforting and nurturing for your baby and even enhances their childhood independence, whilst your breastmilk also contains important fatty acids that help to develop your baby's brain.

Breastfeeding even benefits society because:

  • it's environmentally friendly - breastmilk needs far fewer natural resources to make and produces no waste.
  • breastfed babies are healthier - they are less likely to need medical attention which reduces the cost to governments, insurers and healthcare services. It also means families can spend more time at work instead of taking sick days to look after sick children.

Breastmilk: what to expect

Colostrum or 'first milk'
During pregnancy, your breasts will develop in preparation for breastfeeding and will begin storing colostrum or 'first milk', which will be ready for your baby to drink at birth. Colostrum is your baby's perfect first food.

Colostrum is:

  • thick, sticky and golden yellow in colour
  • rich in protein and secretory immunoglobulin A (IgA) - an antibody which takes over from the antibody that protects your baby via their blood circulation during pregnancy (IgG). IgA boosts your baby's immune system by lining the mucous membranes in their throat, lungs and intestines (which are very leaky) with a barrier that stops most foreign substances from being absorbed.
  • high in white blood cells - to help defend your baby against bacteria and viruses.
  • easy for your baby to digest
  • made in very small quantities - your newborn baby's tummy is only the size of a marble!

Because your baby's tummy is so small when they are first born, it's important to keep feeding them small amounts, often. This ensures they get all the nutrition they need. Feeding frequently also stimulates your breasts to make more milk and set up your milk supply.

When breastmilk comes in
Between days 3-5, colostrum will start to transition into mature breastmilk, a process that continues until two weeks after birth. The quantity increases rapidly, and the milk becomes less sticky and changes to a more white or opaque colour. You may even notice that at around days 3-5 your breasts will suddenly become very full and firm!

As your breasts increase the amount of milk they make, your baby keeps growing, as does their tummy. By days 3-5 your baby's tummy should be the size of a golf ball but by day 10, it will have reached the size of a chicken egg!

Milk supply
By suckling at your breasts, your baby sends powerful messages to your body to make more milk ready for their next feed. This is controlled by two main hormones - prolactin and oxytocin - which are released into your bloodstream when your baby stimulates the nerve endings in your nipples by sucking.

Prolactin helps to make milk and build your supply while oxytocin makes the milk to spurt into your baby's mouth - known as the let-down reflex. The more often you have a let-down reflex and the more milk that is removed from your breasts, the more milk will be made.

Provided you feed your baby when they're hungry, they are attached on properly and you let them feed for as long as they need to feed, your body will adapt to providing just what your baby needs.

It is worth keeping in mind that your milk supply can take around 6 weeks to establish and if anything interrupts this process, it might start to decrease, so it's best to avoid:

  • trying to feed by the clock instead of when your baby is hungry
  • giving formula or water instead of a breastfeed
  • limiting the length or frequency of feeds in any way
  • giving your baby a dummy or teat to suck instead of a breastfeed

It's also a good idea not to schedule any major jobs for the first 6-8 weeks so you can just rest, breastfeed and enjoy your baby.

The day of birth: getting to know your baby

It's almost time to meet your baby! So what can you expect?

Skin-to-skin contact
Provided everything is as expected at birth, your baby should be immediately be lifted up and placed on your chest or tummy for skin-to-skin contact. This skin-to-skin contact helps your baby to:

  • maintain healthy body temperature
  • produce fewer stress hormones
  • breastfeed more easily
  • breathe more easily
  • stabilise their blood sugar levels

Skin-to-skin contact also ensures that your baby feels secure and stimulates hormones that help to start breastfeeding, in both you and your baby, so your baby can begin to breastfeed as soon as they're ready.

Before your baby is born, it's a good idea to ask your midwife or obstetrician if they support immediate skin-to-skin contact with your baby after birth. If they don't (and immediate skin-to-skin contact with your baby is something that you would like) this gives you some time to discuss this further with them and emphasise its importance to you.

Your baby's senses

Sight
Your baby's vision will be a bit blurred when they are born but they will love staring at your face as you hold them!

The distance from your breast to your face is often called 'cuddle distance' because this is the best distance for a baby to be able to focus on your face. As you talk to your baby after the birth, they will make the connection between your face and your voice which will be so familiar from the womb.

Although your newborn won't be able to see colours straight away, they will love looking at objects and images of high contrast, such as things that are black and white or in red, blue and yellow. Read more about play ideas for your newborn.

Hearing
By 20 weeks of pregnancy, your unborn baby can hear all sorts of sounds - your voice, heartbeat and digestion noises, as well as the music you listen to. In fact, newborn babies can usually recognise music they heard frequently when they were still in the womb!

Music and stories are a great way to start connecting with your baby during pregnancy. Mums are lucky because their baby will hear them all day long but your partner and other family members who will be around your baby after birth can also bond by talking and singing to your belly regularly. It doesn't have to be anything special - even talking about the football or cricket is fine - it all helps your baby to get to know the sound of your voices!

Taste
By the second trimester of pregnancy, your baby can taste your individual body odours and even taste some of the flavours of your diet through the amniotic fluid they're swallowing in utero. Eating healthily during your pregnancy and including lots of vegetables and fruit in your diet, will help your baby get used to these foods.

Once born, your baby will be drawn to the sweetish flavour of your breastmilk and it, along with your sweat, will remind your baby of the tastes they experienced in the womb.

Smell
Your baby's sense of smell is very sharp at birth so your individual odour should be very familiar! To strengthen this attachment, many women avoid wearing strong perfumes or using strong smelling soaps after giving birth.

Touch
In late pregnancy, your baby is tucked up snuggly inside your uterus so you can imagine how, at birth, your baby may suddenly feel insecure when there is suddenly so much room in this bright and cold atmosphere for their arms and legs to flail about! Holding your baby skin-to-skin helps them to feel safe again.

Being held securely, tasting and smelling the familiarity of your body, hearing your voice and focussing on your face, helps your baby to slowly adapt to the world. If you're not able to do this immediately, being held skin-to-skin by your partner or just being swaddled, are other ways you can help your baby to feel secure.

Knowing when to feed your baby
Every baby is different so the key is to learn your baby's signals for feeding. Watch your baby so you can quickly learn to recognise their hungry signs.

You should feed your baby whenever they're hungry and for as long as they want to feed. For most newborn babies, this means a total of 8-12 feeds over 24 hours, although some may need more than this and some may need less. Needing several feeds on top of one another, or 'cluster feeding', is also quite normal.

If, for medical reasons, you are unable to breastfeed your baby soon after birth, ask your midwife to help you hand express a few mLs of colostrum so you (or a family member) can feed your baby via a spoon.

Practicalities of breastfeeding

If you were to start a new job or move to a new country, you wouldn't expect to adjust immediately - having a baby, becoming a mum and breastfeeding is no different. Although natural, breastfeeding is a learnt skill that takes time and practice but there is plenty of support available to help you.

Many women find that if they invest time in their new role as a mum and plan nothing else for the first couple of months, transition to motherhood goes more smoothly.

To give you the most time to focus on being a mum to your baby, try to avoid anything major in the first 6-8 weeks, such as:

  • building a house or putting in an extension (unless you can move out)
  • taking on large or stressful projects like planning a wedding or sorting out your finances
  • moving house or living with friends/family members who get under your skin
  • things that require sticking to a schedule - haircuts, dentist appointments, manicures etc - try to do these routine things before you give birth because life with a new baby can be unpredictable!

Before your baby arrives you might wonder how certain aspects of breastfeeding work, for example:

  • whether there are different positions for breastfeeding
  • how exactly your baby will attach to your breast
  • when to feed your baby
  • how to tell if they're getting enough breastmilk
  • whether you'll need any equipment
  • when you might be able to return to (or start) work

Feeding positions
Your baby can be breastfed in many different positions! It will take time to figure out what works for you and your baby and even then, what works on one day might be different on another.

The main thing is that your baby needs to be positioned chest-to-chest with you, so they don't have to turn their neck to suck. Your baby's head also needs to be gently extended so they can drink freely.

A good motto to remember is 'chest-to-chest, nose-to-nipple'.

Some of the most popular feeding positions are:

  • the cradle hold
  • the football hold
  • lying down on your side
  • the cross cradle hold / cross over hold

Your baby's attachment
Getting your baby to attach onto your breast properly is important for preventing your nipples from getting too sore and building up your milk supply. To do so, your baby needs to open their mouth wide and take in a large portion of your breast, so your nipple reaches right back to the junction of their hard and soft palate - just like in this video.

It may seem straightforward but chances are that you and your baby may need to learn how to work together before you start getting a good attachment.

Learn more about and watch a video of tips on how to achieve a good attachment.

Breastfeeding in public
Every mum has the right to meet her baby's needs, wherever they are. If your baby is hungry, you shouldn't be forced to ignore or delay your baby from feeding just because you're in public.

In fact, it is legal to breastfeed in public in Australia anywhere and at any time, without having to ask anyone. It might take some getting used to, but it's important that you feel comfortable and confident wherever you breastfeed.

Planning and building support
Successful breastfeeding depends on the right support from family and friends, and guidance when needed from professionals in the community. Talk to your partner and support people about your plans for feeding your baby so that they know how best to support you. As a new mum you'll need all the help you can get, so don't be afraid to ask for help whenever you can and remember, little jobs like housework can always be done by someone else ...or wait!

Many women find it helpful to learn more about breastfeeding while they're still pregnant. Before your baby arrives, you might like to have a look at the following fantastic resources:

  • Raising Children website
  • Australian Breastfeeding Association

Once your baby is here, you will be feeding around the clock and so it's not uncommon for your partner to feel a bit left out, especially when you're breastfeeding. But they needn't be - there are many other ways your partner can support you with breastfeeding. Even just being enthusiastic and supportive while you learn how to breastfeed can work wonders. Read more about how partners can help with breastfeeding.

There is also heaps of professional support and guidance available and no question is ever silly, don't ever be afraid to ask for help if you need it! Learn more about the different types of support available.

Pumps, dummies and teats
There's so much equipment out there - how do you know what you might need? Besides, if you plan to breastfeed your baby, you might wonder what, if any of it, you actually might need.

The good news is that the only equipment you'll need to breastfeed is your breasts and your baby! That said, there are many products on the market that you may find helpful.

You may also have heard that a dummy is a great way to keep your baby contented. And whilst there is nothing wrong with using a dummy, it's important to realise that babies get unsettled for lots of reasons, so it's always best to try to work out why your baby is crying. It's also not recommended to use a dummy (or a bottle) until your milk supply is established (which can take up to 6-8 weeks).

Most mums find it helpful to be able to express (draw out) some of their breastmilk at some stage, even just to have as a back-up or to relieve some pressure from their breasts.

Expressing isn't as good as your baby is at getting milk out of your breasts (so it can't be used to 'measure' how much milk you make) and there are many ways to do it, some of which include using a breast pump.

While you're still pregnant, there is no need to buy a pump or bottles and teats because all you need to start breastfeeding is you and your baby.

Expressing and storing breastmilk is also not recommended until after 6-8 weeks to ensure you and your baby have a chance to get breastfeeding fully established first. This will also help to make sure you are feeding often enough to establish your milk supply.

Learn more about what's involved with expressing and storing your breastmilk.

Returning to (or starting) work
Returning to, or starting work after having a baby is a big decision, especially if you're still breastfeeding and want to continue.

In Australia, you have a right to breastfeed or express milk anywhere, including at work, but there are still many things to consider. Learn more about working as a breastfeeding mum.

Your baby's first week

The day your baby is finally born will likely be a huge emotional mix of excitement and exhaustion, so it's a good idea to know ahead of time what to expect from your baby in their first week.

Poo and wee
On the first day, your baby will produce poo called 'meconium' that looks a bit like thick, sticky Vegemite - this is normal! Don't worry, as your baby starts getting colostrum through their system, their poo will become less sticky and much easier to clean up.

Keeping an eye on what's in your baby's nappy is a great way to tell whether they're getting enough breastmilk. Over the first week, your baby should be producing progressively more wet nappies soaked through with clear or pale yellow wee.

The number of expected wet nappies that your baby should produce each day should match the number of days since your baby's birth, so expect 1 wet nappy on day 1, 2 on day 2, 3 on day 3 etc, up until your baby is producing at least 6-8 wet nappies soaked through from day 6 onwards.

Your baby may produce a few soft poos or bowel movements every day, although fully breastfed babies might only poo every few days - it varies quite a bit. As the week goes by, your baby's poos should begin to change:

  • day 1: black, thick and sticky 'meconium'
  • days 2-3: gradually getting less sticky and brown/greenish
  • days 3-5 onwards: soft and runny pale yellow or yellowish-brown mustard-coloured poos

Normal newborn weight changes
After you have had skin-to-skin contact and the first breastfeed on the day your baby is born, your midwife will offer to weigh your baby to record their birth weight. It is normal for babies to lose up to 10% of their birth weight in the first few days but they usually regain their birth weight again by week 2-3.

Knowing your baby's birth weight is a good way to monitor their growth and weight gain, and therefore that they're feeding well. If your baby loses more than 10% of their birth weight or is not tracking well on the growth chart, your midwife may give you individualised feeding advice.

Rooming-in
For the first 6-12 months, it is recommended to keep your baby in a cot in your room as this has been shown to reduce the risk of sudden unexpected death in infancy (SUDI), including SIDS.

Also called 'rooming-in', keeping your baby in your room with you all the time will help you get to know your baby quickly. You will start to recognise their unique facial expressions, sounds and body movements which will help you to understand what they're trying to communicate, for example, are they hungry or tired?

Rooming-in allows you to respond to your baby appropriately and to breastfeed them whenever they are hungry. This is one of the reasons that rooming-in makes breastfeeding easier and helps you to build and maintain your milk supply.

Babies who room-in with their mums tend to:

  • cry less
  • settle more quickly (which means more sleep for you!)
  • take in more breastmilk

Not surprisingly, rooming-in is now standard in almost all hospitals and birth centres in Australia for mums and healthy term babies.

Breastfeeding over the first 6-8 weeks

Surviving the first week with your new baby is no mean feat, so well done! Breastfeeding will probably still be a case of trial and error to figure out what works best for you and your baby but the important thing is that you don't give up!

Successful breastfeeding depends on the right support from family and friends, and there is plenty of professional support available (much of it free) to help you breastfeed your baby if you need it.

Making enough milk
Breastfeeding is a learning curve for you and for your baby - after 6-8 weeks you will feel much more confident. Just think of it as a new job - you need to learn the ropes!

And even if you (or your own mum) have had trouble breastfeeding before, every baby is different, so it doesn't mean you will have the same problems again with this baby. Even if you do, don't worry, plenty of help is available!

Follow these simple tips to ensure you make enough breastmilk for your baby in the early weeks:

  • make sure you feed your baby whenever they're hungry and for as long as they want to feed
  • don't try to force your baby into a routine or feed by the clock - your milk supply will suffer and your baby is likely to cry a lot
  • avoid giving your baby any supplements, water, teats or dummies

And remember:

  • your baby will need to feed frequently - for most newborn babies, this means a total of around 8-12 feeds over a 24 hour period
  • several feeds on top of one another, or 'cluster feeds', is normal
  • newborn babies have tiny tummies and need to feed throughout the night as well as the day

Skin-to-skin contact
Provided your baby was well at birth, they should have been immediately placed on your chest or tummy for skin-to-skin contact but if a medical reason prevented this from happening at birth, try to resume it as soon as possible. It is never too late to start!

How will I know if my baby is getting enough milk in the early weeks?
After the first week, some good signs that your baby is getting enough milk are that your baby:

  • has at least 6-8 wet nappies soaked through with clear or pale yellow wee per day
  • passes soft and runny pale yellow or yellowish-brown mustard-coloured poos
  • is mostly settled between feeds - don't worry, some fussy or crying periods are normal
  • is growing well and gaining weight - your midwife or local health nurse will guide you
  • has good skin colour and muscle tone
