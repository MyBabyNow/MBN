Poo and wee

Having a baby means you'll be seeing lots of poo and wee! Knowing how to examine these is a way of checking your baby's health, so here are a few things to expect:

  • for the first few days after birth, your baby should pass green/black, thick sticky poos called 'meconium'
  • after the first week, your baby should start to pass pale yellow or yellowish-brown poos
    • fully breastfed babies might poo every day or every few days - it varies a lot
  • at least 6-8 wet nappies soaked through with clear or pale yellow wee per day is a good sign that your baby is getting enough fluid
  • as your baby is introduced to different solids, their poo will change in colour, smell and texture and it may also contain undigested food as their digestive system is still developing

Not sure if that disposable nappy is full?
Try feeling how heavy it is, rather than how wet it is. If there are little crystals inside the nappy, don't worry, that's from the nappy, not your baby!

Your baby's wee

Colour What it means
Light pink or orange Normal
Dark Your baby might not be feeding enough or is dehydrated
Red or brown blood) Speak to your GP or maternal and child health nurse as soon as possible

Your baby's poo (breastfed babies only)

Description What it means
Soft and runny Normal
Mustard colour Normal
Green colour Your baby might be over- or under-feeding; taking in too much lactose (the natural sugar in breastmilk) from your earlier breastmilk ('foremilk') and not getting enough of the creamier milk ('hindmilk') at the end of a feed - make sure your baby drains your breast well when feeding (try offering the same side twice); or have a tummy bug.
Smells sweet Normal - the smell can also be affected by what you've eaten
Hard and dry Your baby might be constipated - speak to your GP or maternal and child health nurse
Runnier than usual Your baby might have diarrhoea - speak to your GP or maternal and child health nurse and be careful that your baby doesn't get dehydrated
White, grey or pale yellow colour If your baby also has jaundice, this can be a sign of liver problems - speak to your GP or maternal and child health nurse
Blood in poo Speak to your GP or maternal and child health nurse as soon as possible. It may just be because your baby is constipated, but it's good to get it checked out.
