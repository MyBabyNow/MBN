Feeding positions and attachment

Different feeding positions

Important tips for any position you choose:

  • 'tummy to mummy' - your baby should face your breast, with their head, shoulders and body in a straight line
  • 'nose to nipple' - your baby's nose or top lip should be opposite your nipple
  • 'chest to breast' - your baby should be brought to your breast instead of moving your breast towards your baby

Support your baby's neck with your thumb and forefinger, rather than cradling their head in your hand. This is to avoid pushing your baby's head into the breast. Your baby's head needs to be extended in order to drink properly.

Your baby can be breastfed in different positions. The most popular positions to feed a baby are:

  • the cradle hold
    • support your baby in the crook of your arm (left arm when feeding on the left side)
    • better for babies delivered vaginally and over one month old - it can be hard to guide a newborn onto your nipple properly in this hold

  • the cross cradle hold / cross over hold
    • supported your baby by the opposite arm to your breast (right arm holding when feeding on the left side)
    • better for smaller babies and babies having trouble attaching - you can guide them more easily in this hold
  • the football hold
    • supported your baby by the opposite arm to your breast (right arm holding when feeding on the left side)
    • better for smaller babies and babies having trouble attaching - you can guide them more easily in this hold
  • lying down on your side
    • you lie on your side and your baby lies on their side facing you - try to keep your back straight and bring baby to your breast
    • a good position if you're feeling exhausted and want to lie down or sitting up is uncomfortable

Your baby's attachment

Getting your baby to attach onto your breast properly will help you build up your milk supply and prevent your nipples from getting too sore (though some soreness for the first few days is normal). You and your baby may need to learn how to work together to get a good attachment. If you feel pain well after your baby first attaches or don't think they are attaching properly, ask for help. Your maternal and child health nurse, midwife, a lactation consultant, or the ABA helpline are good places to start.

Baby-led attachment

Baby-led attachment is lets your baby find your breast by themselves, before attaching and starting to feed. It's best to try this when your baby is relaxed and you have skin-to-skin contact.
