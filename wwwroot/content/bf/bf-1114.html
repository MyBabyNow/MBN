Food tips for mums

Food needs: what you need to eat
It may surprise you that your body needs more food while you're breastfeeding than when you were pregnant - that's because your body burns through energy (kilojoules/calories) and nutrients making milk for your baby. When you're breastfeeding, your body absorbs and uses nutrients from food better than usual, and uses some of the fat stores from when you were pregnant (which is why breastfeeding can help you lose weight).

Eating enough to look after yourself and your baby will help you stay at your best health while adjusting to being a mum. This doesn't mean eating more chocolate, chips or other 'junk' food, it means you need to eat more from the five major food groups we need for everyday health.

Read more on healthy eating and serving sizes for mums.

Feeling thirsty?
You might find that you get extra thirsty when you are breastfeeding your baby. Keep hydrated by drinking lots of water - keep a reusable water bottle by your side to remind you to drink it (instead of less healthy drinks with added sugar).

Try to limit drinks containing caffeine and alcohol, as these pass through your breastmilk into your baby's system and can affect their behaviour and development.

Supplements
You should be able to get most of what you need when breastfeeding from a well-balanced healthy diet. The only nutrients you may need help with are iodine and vitamin D. It is recommended that breastfeeding mums take an iodine supplement and that your GP checks your vitamin D level to see if you, or your baby, needs a supplement.

Just remember that any medication (herbal or other) will have side effects that you need to know about - you should speak with your GP for guidance.

See the Australian Dietary Guidelines for more information on what and how much to eat.

Quick food ideas
Eating enough food and drinking enough water throughout the day can be surprisingly tough for mums! Being busy looking after their babies, mums often forget to eat, or end up eating on the run.

Here are some ideas on foods you can quickly eat throughout the day that are healthy for you:

  • plain raw vegetables - carrot, celery, cucumber, capsicum and broccoli are all yummy raw. Dip into unsweetened plain natural yoghurt or hummus for some extra flavour
  • plain fruit - keep a bowl of fruit out so you remember to eat two pieces a day!
  • nuts - unless you are allergic, nuts are the perfect high energy snack packed with nutrition - choose unsalted ones and add a little bit of dried fruit like sultanas for extra flavour and energy
  • wholegrain toast - stick to grainy types of bread and add some unsalted peanut butter or other nut spread, avocado, margarine, or a slice of cheese on top for a very quick meal or snack
  • throw a slice of cheese on top of a cracker for a gourmet snack - look for crackers that are lower in salt (aim for < 400mg/100g) and include wholegrains
  • open up a can of tuna in springwater, mix through some low-fat mayonnaise and spread on bread - yum!
  • boiled eggs - quick and easy to prepare and you can eat them as they are, or spread on bread
  • untoasted muesli - who says muesli has to be for breakfast? Eat a bowl of untoasted muesli with low-fat milk at any time of the day as a quick and filling meal

Food and breastmilk
Breastfeeding doesn't mean you have to have a 'perfect' diet - eating well is more important for your own health and energy levels.

What you eat doesn't really affect the:

  • amount of milk you make
  • protein or lactose content of your milk
  • the risk of your baby getting asthma, eczema, intolerances or other allergies

Many mums get worried about whether certain foods in their diet affect their baby and although some foods might slightly alter the taste and/or smell of your breastmilk, this is not really anything to worry about.

While some babies can react to traces of food in breastmilk, it's best to seek advice from a paediatrician specialising in allergies before you make any changes to your diet. Babies can be unsettled for many reasons and your breastmilk may not necessarily be the problem.

Read more about intolerance and allergies in babies.

What you eat does affect the:

  • levels of vitamins and minerals in your milk
  • types of fat in your milk

If you are on a special or restricted diet (such as paleo or vegan) you should speak to your GP or a dietitian to make sure both you and your baby stay healthy while you breastfeed.

Caffeine
Caffeine passes through your breastmilk into your baby - the amount transferred depends on how your body handles caffeine. It takes about 1 hour after consuming caffeine for the amount of caffeine in your breastmilk to peak.

So if you had a coffee at 10:30am, it would take until about 11:30am before the majority of the caffeine from that coffee appears in your breastmilk.

How fast your baby can process caffeine depends on how old they are:

  • newborns can take between 2-4 days to get rid of half the caffeine they take in
  • by about 3-4 months it only takes a few hours for babies to process caffeine

Caffeine can upset some babies, so limiting your caffeine intake is a good idea. There are mixed ideas on how much caffeine a breastfeeding mum should have, but in Australia having no more than 300mg a day (less if you also smoke) is recommended.
