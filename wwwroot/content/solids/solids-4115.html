Top tips

Starting your baby on solids is an exciting milestone... but it can also be a time of frustration and meal time battles!

Use our top tips to try to make the process as smooth as possible and give your baby the healthiest possible start.

Keep feeding your baby breastmilk/formula until 12 months

Even though it is recommended that solids are introduced from around 6 months, breastmilk or formula are still important sources of nutrients for your baby until at least 12 months. The only thing that changes is which is offered first - milk or solids.

After 12 months, replacing formula with regular full cream cow's milk is fine - not low and reduced fat though, as these do not have enough energy (kilojoules/calories) for your baby's rapidly growing body. By now, your baby's main nutrition should be coming from family meals instead of milk.

Cow's milk should not be given before 12 months as it does not contain enough iron and can overwork your baby's kidneys, which are still developing.

Promote healthy habits from the start

The foods we offer to our babies influences the foods they learn to like, and eat, as they get older.

If you want your baby to develop good eating habits right from the start, be sure to:

  • set a good example for your baby
  • provide your baby with healthy first foods
  • include your baby in your 'meal time' routine

Remember, it takes time for babies to learn to accept and like some foods, especially vegetables. Some resources you might find useful:

  • top tips to help your baby like vegetables
  • our vegetable tracker to keep track of which vegetables you have offered to your baby and how they reacted
  • how to handle fussy eaters
  • our feeding practices quiz for personalised feedback on how you are feeding your baby

'Everyday' and 'Sometimes' foods
Rather than label foods as 'good' or 'bad', it can be useful to think about foods for everyday and sometimes foods.

Everyday foods supply important nutrients and help to keep your baby (and you!) healthy and well. Everyday foods fit into the 5 major food groups:

  • Vegetables and legumes/beans
  • Fruit
  • Grain (cereal) foods, mostly wholegrain
  • Lean meat and poultry, fish, eggs, nuts and seeds, and legumes/beans
  • Milk, yoghurt, cheese and/or alternatives

By offering your baby a variety of these foods every day, you'll be giving your baby the best chance at optimal growth and development and helping your baby form healthy eating habits from the start.

Sometimes foods (which we often call 'junk foods') are full of 'empty' energy and offer very little (if any) nutrients. Sometimes foods include foods such as chocolate, chips, biscuits, cakes, ice cream and lollies (and alcohol for the adults).

These foods make babies (and us!) feel full, so they tend to replace the more nutritious everyday foods when we eat them, which is why we should only eat them sometimes.

Giving your baby sometimes foods:

  • means your baby will be too full to eat the healthy foods you offer and will get less of the important nutrients they need
  • can affect your baby's growth when given too often - they may gain too much weight or develop nutrient deficiencies, in some cases
  • can cause tooth decay (especially sugary foods) - even before the teeth have come through
  • can be a choking risk - small, hard or round lollies should be avoided in your baby's first 12 months

Once your baby is 12 months old, it's OK for them to try these food but try to keep these for special occasions and don't keep them at home. There will be plenty of occasions for them to have sometimes foods at birthday parties and other celebrations, so there is no need to worry about them 'missing out'!

When they are old enough to understand, you can then talk to them about everyday and sometimes foods, but remember not to label them as good and bad.

It's also a good idea to talk to your family members (and anyone else who looks after your baby) about why you would prefer that they don't give your baby sometimes foods - hopefully this will stop them giving your baby these foods when you're not around.

No need to reward your baby with food

Babies pick up habits from a young age and these often carry on as they grow older. To avoid having your child always associate receiving food as a reward, it is important that you do not give your baby any food for being 'good'.

Things to be mindful of:

  • when serving something sweet, try not to make a fuss about it being 'yummy' or a special reward
  • try not to be overly strict around sweets (such as using them to reward) - this can make your baby more excited and end up promoting this food to become a favourite
  • babies may demand sometimes foods and/or sweets - this may become a way your baby tries to take control over what they eat (a potential 'battle ground'!); try not to keep these foods at home and avoid the battles!

Instead of rewarding your baby's good behaviour with food, try to think of non-food rewards that your child may like. Some ideas include:

  • games
  • stories
  • a new toy or sticker
  • taking a trip to the park

Babies know their own appetite

Babies are excellent at knowing when they are hungry and when they have had enough. Their appetite can change from day-to-day but it also changes in response to how much they are growing at the time.

Things to look out for:

  • watch their facial expressions and body language when feeding
  • just like us, your baby will eat more on some days than other days - it's not always about the food offered
  • your baby will tell you when they want more and when they have had enough

It's important that you don't force your baby to finish all the food you offer if they don't want to. Letting your baby learn to trust their appetite from an early age will help them not to overeat when they are older.

Food to avoid feeding your baby

Remember to wait until your baby is 12 months old until you offer:

  • whole nuts, raw or hard foods like carrots or apple to avoid choking
  • fluids like cow's and goat's milks, juices (including 100% fruit juice), soft drinks, cordials, sport drinks, teas (including herbal) and coffee - these are not suitable for babies under 12 months
  • honey - this can cause botulism (a serious bacterial infection) in babies under 12 months

Read more about what foods to offer and which should be avoided and don't forget to take our baby food quiz and our feeding practices quiz to see if you're on the right track!

Try not to fuss

The more you fuss about food, the more your baby will fuss. You may be tempted to give your baby a 'favourite' food if they refuse the food you have offered but this can lead to fussy eating and can make it harder for you in the long run.

Instead, if your baby has refused a food or meal, simply take it away, without comment or fuss, and remain calm.

Don't forget that it can take up to 10-15 exposures to a food before your baby will accept it, so try offering it again at the next regular meal or snack time, maybe even prepared in a different way.

And remember, how you react to, and handle, food refusal is very important in avoiding meal time battles down the track!

Your baby's food doesn't have to cost a fortune

Just like with adult food, packaged food can be expensive and less healthy. To save money and still give your baby a variety of healthy food, you can feed your baby meals cooked for the family.

Just make sure you remove your baby's serving before adding any sugar, salt (including stocks), extra fats or oils, and don't forget to adjust the texture to what your baby can tolerate.

Learn more about preparing food for your baby.

Avoid adding extra sugar, salt, fats or oils

Unless your GP or maternal and child health nurse has advised you to do otherwise, avoid adding salt (including stocks), sugar, fats or oils to your baby's food.

These can damage your baby's health and give them a taste for foods that are less healthy, which can lead to further health problems in the future.

Learn more about preparing food for your baby.

Offer a variety of healthy foods

Babies can learn to like different healthy foods so make sure there are a variety of healthy foods at each feed.

Remember:

  • it can take up to 10-15 tries of a new food, so even if your baby keeps rejecting a healthy food, it is worth persisting to see if your baby will eventually accept it
  • food preferences do not run in families so even if you don't like it, this doesn't mean that your baby won't
