When to introduce solids

When to introduce solids
It is recommended that you introduce solids when your baby shows signs that they are ready. These signs happen at different times for different babies, but most babies will show signs around six months of age. Until then, breastmilk or formula gives them all the nutrition they need.

Check if your baby is ready for solids by taking our quiz.

From around 6 months your baby:

  • will need nutrients that breastmilk/formula alone can't provide,such as iron, zinc, vitamin D and extra energy (kilojoules/calories)
  • should start showing signs of wanting to try new foods and enjoy trying new textures

How can I tell when my baby is ready for solid foods

  • holding their head up and able to sit with some support
  • reaching out/opening mouth and/or putting their hands in their mouth when you eat
  • they are still showing hungry signs after a breast/formula feed
  • no longer stick tongue out when food is put in their mouth (‘extrusion reflex’)
  • chewing and biting movements

Check if your baby is ready for solids by taking our quiz.

Why wait until around 6 months to start solids?

For the first 6 months of your baby’s life, breastmilk or formula provide all of the nutrients your baby needs. Even once they have started solids, breastmilk or formula will still continue to be an important source of nutrients until your baby is at least 12 months.

Reasons why you should avoid introducing solids before 6 months:

  • your baby's tummy is small – starting solids too early can make your baby too full to have breastmilk/formula as well, making it more likely that they will miss out on the vital nutrients and energy (kilojoules/calories) they need to grow and develop properly
  • your baby's body is still developing physically – it cannot yet manage solid foods (such as being able to swallow different textured solids properly); if your baby pushes out any solid foods placed on their tongue, rather than this being a rejection of the food, it’s actually a natural reflex to having objects in their mouth – this ‘extrusion reflex’ means they are not yet ready for solids, but the good news is that it normally disappears by 6 months of age
  • if you’re breastfeeding, it can affect your milk supply – giving your baby other foods and fluids can reduce the amount of breastmilk you make
  • it can upset your baby’s tummy – solid foods are less sterile than breastmilk/formula and your because baby’s immune system is still maturing, increasing their exposure to pathogens in foods can cause upset tummies and/or diarrhoea

When to start

It is recommended that you wait until 6 months before introducing solid foods to your baby’s diet. This gives your baby’s body time to develop to a stage where it is ready to manage solids and ensures that they are still getting all the nutrition they need from breastmilk/formula.

Your baby’s first solid foods should be high in iron. This is because, from 6 months, your baby’s iron needs are greater than what your breastmilk or formula can provide.

What foods to try (and avoid)

The order or number of foods introduced at a time doesn't really matter, just as long as your baby is getting iron rich foods that are mashed/puréed down to a suitable texture for their age.

It won’t be long before your baby will be eating much the same foods as the rest of the family, just at a texture that suits them!

Foods to try

Iron rich foods that make great first foods include:

  • iron fortified baby cereals
  • meat
  • fish
  • chicken
  • legumes (such as baked beans or lentils)
  • leafy green vegetables

Foods to add later:

  • vegetables
  • fruits
  • cheese
  • full fat unsweetened plain natural yoghurt

You should even give your baby foods that often considered as allergenic, such as:

  • peanut butter
  • cooked egg
  • dairy
  • wheat products

This is true, even if your baby is at high risk of allergy. Just keep an eye out for any reactions (such as a rash or upset tummy) and if you do notice one, or are not sure, stop that food and contact your GP. Learn more about allergies and how to avoid them.

What to avoid in the first 12 months

Choking hazards:

  • whole nuts
  • raw foods
  • hard foods

Foods your baby’s body is not ready for:

  • honey – this can cause botulism (a serious bacterial infection) in babies under 12 months
  • cow’s and goat’s milks (except in small quantities in cooking)
  • additions such as sugar, salt (including stocks), extra fats and oils

Treat or sometimes foods which can fill your baby up without providing much (or any) nutritional value, leaving little room for the nutritious foods your baby needs for optimal growth and development. Avoid giving your baby a taste for these foods (and making it harder to encourage healthy eating habits later on) by not offering:

  • chocolate
  • chips
  • biscuits
  • cakes
  • ice cream
  • lollies

Until 12 months, the only drinks your baby should be having are breastmilk or formula and small amounts of cooled boiled water; avoid giving your baby other drinks such as:

  • juices (including 100% fruit juice)
  • soft drinks
  • cordials
  • sports drinks
  • tea (including herbal)
  • coffee

How, when and what textures?

How and when

The first thing to keep in mind is that you provide the food to your baby and your baby decides if they will eat it and if so, how much. A good motto to remember is: parents provide, babies decide.

Just like the rest of us, all babies are different, so some will enjoy solids faster than others will. At first, your baby will probably end up spitting most foods out!

Although messy, letting your baby explore their food with their fingers (including purées) allows them to smell, feel and hopefully even taste new foods – it’s all part of the learning experience!


To help you get started:

  • Until at least 8 months, start with offering solids twice a day after their normal breastmilk/formula feed so they still get the nutrients they need from the milk before filling up on solids.
  • Sit at the same level as your baby and try not to fuss – if they spit out or refuse to accept the food, just try again another time.
  • Offer solids when both you and your baby are happy and relaxed.
  • Allow your baby to sit with you when you are eating or at family meal times, such as breakfast.
  • Over the first few weeks, gradually increase how often you offer solids.

Textures To help you get started:

Just as a variety of different foods is important, so is a variety of age-appropriate textures. In the past, most babies had to stick to puréed foods for a longer time because they started solids before they were ready but when babies start solids at around 6 months, they can begin learning about other textures fairly quickly. Varying the food texture helps your baby learn to chew (even without teeth!) as well as to swallow.

Start with puréed or smooth food and gradually progress to lumpier foods as soon as your baby tolerates it. These lumpier foods will help teach your baby how to chew their food (no teeth required) which then helps them to speak.

Generally, if your baby is:

  • 6–8 months – use only puréed and mashed foods but move to minced (or mashed with a fork) and chopped foods soon
  • 8 months – begin finger foods
  • 12 months – your baby might even be ready to start eating foods with the same textures enjoyed by the rest of the family

Just make sure you introduce lumpy food before your baby is 10 months of age to avoid feeding difficulties down the track, such as only accepting smooth or soft foods.

How much solid food should you offer?
At first, your baby will probably only eat very small amounts. Learning how to swallow new textures is just like any other new skill – it takes time to master!

Try:

  • starting with a ½ teaspoon twice a day, until your baby is used to the different texture
  • then gradually increasing to a few tablespoons per feed over the next few weeks
  • giving your baby some finger foods – soft pieces of food that your baby can hold and try feeding to themselves

As long as you provide your baby with healthy solid food options, they will decide if, and how much of it, they will eat.

Signs of being hungry for solids (and full)

Signs that your baby is hungry for solids include:

  • getting excited when they see you getting their food ready
  • leaning towards you while they are sitting in the highchair
  • opening their mouth as you're about to feed them

From 7–8 months, your baby may reach for the spoon or for food when they are hungry, or point to food.

From 8–12 months, your baby may also show excitement when you give them food.


Signs that your baby is no longer hungry include:

Signs that your baby is hungry for solids include:

  • refusing to eat or closing their mouth tightly
  • pushing away the spoon, or your hand
  • turning their head away from you
  • looking upset or starting to crying

If your baby shows these signs, it’s important to follow their cues and not pressure them to eat or complete the meal. This encourages them to trust their own appetite and not to overeat when they are older.

How to deal with food being spat out

Just because your baby spits out new foods, it doesn’t always mean they don’t like them – they just need time to get used to them! In fact, it can take up to 10–15 tries of a new food before your baby may accept it – so try not to give up!

It is normal for babies to spit out new and unknown foods, especially vegetables, which tend to be less sweet and more bitter than other foods. It’s thought that because bitter tastes can be associated with poisons/toxins, this initial rejection of bitter foods is an instinctive reaction to protect against harm.

If your baby spits out a food, the best thing to do is to try to react calmly and take the food away, before moving onto the next activity.

Resist the temptation to offer a different food as this can lead to food battles and fussy eating down the track!

As frustrating as spat out food can be, try to keep in mind that your baby needs time to explore the new texture/taste and they will take more in at each feed as they grow and get more used to solid food.

Preparing and storing homemade baby food

Practise good hygiene

Your baby's immune system is still developing so tummy upsets can easily happen.

Reduce this risk by:

  • always washing your hands well with soap and water (antiseptic rubs don't always kill all the germs) before touching anything for food preparation
  • making sure any equipment to be used (e.g. knife, chopping board, bowl) has been washed well
  • not letting raw foods come into contact with cooked foods
    • always throwing out any leftover food your baby has not eaten from their plate as this can grow harmful bacteria
    • following the manufacturer's instructions for sterilising milk bottles
    • never reheating foods more than once

Baby food is easy to make!

There’s no need to buy packaged food, fancy ingredients, special equipment, or take lessons on how to make food for your baby – just follow these simple steps!

No fancy equipment needed!

A food processor or blender is great for puréeing or mashing your baby's food when first introducing them to solids but if you only have a masher or fork, they are fine too, depending on the texture your baby can tolerate. Add a little liquid (e.g. breastmilk, formula or boiled cooled water) to make the food smoother.

No need to take cooking lessons!

Simply bake, steam, roast or microwave until foods are soft and tender. Just be careful when microwaving and stir wherever possible to avoid ‘hot spots’ that could burn your baby’s mouth or tongue.

No special ingredients needed!
For the most part, you can save yourself time and money by making your baby’s meal from the same ingredients as your own family meal.

Just remember:

  • that your baby’s first solid foods should be high in iron
  • to remove your baby's serving before you add any sugar, salt (including stocks), extra fats and oils
  • you may need to mash or purée their food to begin with but as their chewing improves you can change the consistency to chopping up instead
  • to make sure any fruits and vegetables have been washed well

Preparing the right texture

As well as different types of foods, a variety of textures helps teach your baby different skills such as chewing and swallowing, which also eventually helps them with speaking.

It takes time to learn how to manage different textures, so it’s important that the solids you offer are always prepared to a suitable consistency for your baby.

Start with puréed/finely mashed food before moving onto lumpier food, and finger foods (from 8 months). You should ideally do this before 10 months to avoid feeding difficulties down the track such as only accepting smooth or soft foods.

Purées/finely mashed food

Your baby's first solids should include foods high in iron and be prepared to a smooth texture.

Use a blender and add breastmilk, made up formula or boiled cooled water to make the texture thinner.

Once your baby has mastered puréed foods (and is swallowing more than they are spitting out!) it's time to introduce lumpy food.

Extra baby food can be stored

Babies only tend to eat small amounts in a meal, so making extra food ahead of time is a great way of saving you valuable time and energy!

You can do this by:

  • making extra at a meal or preparing a large batch of food
  • freezing any extra food made (or leftovers not served) by putting 1–2 tablespoons of puréed/mashed food in separate blobs on a baking sheet and covering with cling film, or separating into:
    • airtight containers
    • ice cube trays sealed inside a plastic bag (e.g. snaplock bag)
  • transferring the cubes/blobs to a plastic freezer bag once the food is frozen solid
  • labelling the freezer bag with the contents and date it was prepared – this is important because frozen baby food should be used within one month of freezing

When you’re ready to use frozen baby food, simply thaw the food in a closed container (not in an uncovered bowl) in the fridge.

If you need food ready more quickly, you can:

  • fill a large bowl with hot water and put the cube/blob in a small bowl sitting in the hot water – it should be ready in 10–20 minutes
  • use a microwave (as a last resort), making sure you stir the food well to avoid ‘hot spots’ that could burn your baby’s mouth or tongue

Finger foods and baby-led weaning

Finger foods

Finger foods are soft pieces of food, cut up into small pieces enough pieces so that they do not pose a choking risk, which babies can hold and try feeding to themselves.

Giving your baby finger foods is a great way to encourage them to try, and eventually learn how, to feed themselves. Because it often creates a mess, it can be tempting to wait until they are older, but as soon as your baby has progressed to sucking and biting (usually by about 8 months) they will learn to manage finger foods.

Just be mindful to never leave your baby alone while eating – it does not take long for a baby to choke on something! Gagging, on the other hand, is common and is actually a good thing – read more about the differences between choking and gagging

Finger food ideas

Try offering a variety of different colours, textures and tastes to make food more exciting for your baby but keep in mind that finger foods should always be soft foods, cut into small enough pieces so that they do not pose a choking risk.

Some ideas to try:

  • steamed baby corn
  • sliced avocado
  • steamed carrot sticks
  • watermelon cubes
  • strawberries
  • thin rice cakes with a spread (avocado/cheese)
  • halved unseeded grapes
  • chunks of banana
  • cucumber sticks or slices
  • capsicum slices
  • cooked pear slices
  • orange pieces
  • pieces of cooked cauliflower or broccoli

Baby-led weaning

There has been some debate in recent years about the best way to start babies on solids, with the main ways being baby-led weaning (BLW) and traditional spoon-feeding.

BLW is all about letting babies feed themselves and, given their developmental motor skills, this usually means they start by picking up chunks of food that they can chew. Traditional spoon-feeding involves purées and then moving onto chewable solids as the baby gets older.

The trend of BLW has progressed quickly and researchers are still studying whether we should be ‘dropping’ the spoon just yet. For example, it might be harder for babies following BLW to eat enough foods high in iron to prevent iron deficiency, which can delay brain development and may not be reversible.

If you want to try BLW, make sure you still offer your baby high iron foods from the moment you start weaning, such as toast fingers topped with iron fortified baby cereal and strips of cooked red meat.

Packaged baby food

Packaged baby food

Ready-made packaged baby foods can be nutritious, and are certainly very handy when you are short on time, but they also tend to be expensive and often contain extra, unwanted ingredients.

Giving your baby finger foods is a great way to encourage them to try, and eventually learn how, to feed themselves. Because it often creates a mess, it can be tempting to wait until they are older, but as soon as your baby has progressed to sucking and biting (usually by about 8 months) they will learn to manage finger foods.

Some of the problems with this are that:

  • sweetness encourages your baby to get a ‘sweet tooth’, which makes it harder to introduce other foods that aren’t as sweet, such as some vegetables
  • smoothness can make it harder to introduce other textures, such as those that are more lumpy and chunky
  • mixture of different foods doesn’t expose your baby to individual food tastes, which can promote fussy eating later on

Think of bought baby foods like ‘takeaway foods’ – convenient to use now and again, such as when you’re out or short on time, but best not to use them too often.

The great news is that making packaged baby foods at is easy! Making your own packaged baby foods also saves you money and lets you decide exactly what you put in (or leave out), including salt and sugar.

If you find yourself needing to buy some packaged baby food, use these tips to help you choose the best option for your baby.

Top tips for choosing baby foods at the shop:

  • choose products that have the lowest amount of sugar, sodium (salt) and saturated fat
    • compare products using the per 100g or per 100/mL column on the nutrition information panel and choose those with the lowest values
    • you can also look in the ingredients list for added sugar and salt – the closer the ingredient is to the start of the list, the more of that ingredient there is in the product
  • go for items that are high in iron such as meat, fish, chicken, legumes or iron fortified baby cereal, especially if you use packaged foods every day
  • make sure the suggested age on the packet is suitable for your baby …but keep in mind that many baby foods will say ‘suitable from 4 months’ even though it’s recommended to wait until around 6 months to start solids, when most babies are ready
  • opt for whole foods that can be transported easily wherever possible, such as bananas, avocado or cheese sticks
  • yoghurt is a great option but always choose full fat unsweetened plain natural yoghurts to avoid lots of unnecessary added sugar – you can always add your own fruit later for extra sweetness if you want to!
  • double check that the food is still in date – if you plan on keeping the food for a little while before using it, check that the best before date won’t pass before you get to use it
  • not all packaged baby foods are on the shelf – you can find fresh baby meals in the freezer section too

Milk and other drinks

  • which drinks are not suitable and should be avoided
  • how your own drink choices influence your baby’s preferences

Under 6 months of age

The only drinks babies under 6 months need are formula or breastmilk, unless you have been advised otherwise by your GP or maternal and child health nurse. These provide them with all their nutrition needs as well as fluids to prevent dehydration.

From 6–12 months

Breastmilk or formula should still be your baby’s main drink in the first 12 months. A little cow's milk used in cooking is OK but it should not be their main drink.

Small amounts of cooled boiled water can be given from around 6 months if your baby needs extra fluids (e.g. in hot weather). Try not to give water too often because it can fill your baby's little tummy, making them feel too full to drink enough milk and they will risk not getting all the nutrition than need from milk/solids.

Once your baby has reached 6 months, it is also time to encourage your baby to drink (breastmilk, formula or cooled boiled water) from a cup!

After 12 months

From 12 months, until your baby is 2 years or older, regular full cream cow's milk is fine. By now, their main nutrition should be coming from family meals instead of milk. Avoid giving low and reduced fat cow's milk though – these do not contain enough energy (kilojoules/calories) for your baby’s rapidly growing body.

It’s also best to serve any drinks in a cup (other than when breastfeeding) to help your baby learn to sip.

What about drinks other than milk or water? 

As adults, we enjoy many different drinks, so it can be tempting to offer your baby drinks other than just milk or water. However, the reality is that most other drinks can cause your baby harm, even if they appear to enjoy them.

For example, juices (including 100% fruit juice), soft drinks, cordials or sports and energy drinks can:

  • damage growing teeth – even before they have come through
  • cause your baby to gain too much weight, get tummy upsets and diarrhoea
  • fill up your baby’s tummy, leaving less room for more nutritious milks/foods needed for your baby’s growth and development
  • encourage your baby to develop a ‘sweet tooth’, making it harder to introduce other foods that aren’t as sweet, such as some vegetables
  • be harmful to your baby’s developing body if they contain high levels of caffeine, which is common with energy drinks

Coffee and teas (including herbal), like other drinks, can also fill up your baby’s small tummy (making them less hungry for milks/foods they need for growth and development) but they also should be avoided because they:

  • can prevent important nutrients (such as iron) being absorbed into your baby’s body properly
  • often contain high levels of caffeine which can be harmful for your baby’s development

Be a good role model

Because your baby is always watching you (and trying to copy what you do) they will see the drinks that you, your partner and other carers enjoy, and want to drink those too!

If you regularly drink juice, soft drinks, cordials, sports drinks or energy drinks, now may be time to think about changing your own habits. An added bonus is that by cutting back on these drinks, you’ll save yourself money, boost your own health and probably lose weight at the same time!

Remember, you are your baby’s most important role model – your own drinking habits, as well as the drinks you keep at home, all play a big role in what your baby will want to drink themselves, especially as they get older. It’s important that you set a good example by not offering your baby these drinks, or making them available at home, to avoid a potential battle down the track.

Introduce a sippy cup at 6 months of age

Once your baby reaches 6 months of age, it’s a good idea to start offering your baby’s drinks in a 'sippy' cup – a training cup that helps your baby learn how to sip. They’re usually made from plastic and have a lid with a spout that lets your baby drink without spilling.

The aim is to introduce a cup at 6 months and if you’re bottle feeding, to phase out bottles altogether by 12 months.

Why a cup?

Using a cup increases the movement of your baby’s jaw and chin, which improves the coordination, control and flexibility of their tongue and lips – all of which are important in learning to talk!

It also boosts your baby’s independence and improves their hand coordination

Which sippy cup is best?

There are many different types of sippy cups available! You may need to experiment to find one that suits your baby.

Things to look for when choosing a sippy cup:

  • clear/transparent material so your baby can see the liquid inside
  • two handles to make it easier for your baby to grip and pick up
  • free-flowing that doesn’t have a non-spill valve – these valves make it harder for your baby to get the liquid out and encourages them to suck instead of sip
  • a soft bendable spout
  • be harmful to your baby’s developing body if they contain high levels of caffeine, which is common with energy drinks

As soon as your baby is ready, you can move onto an open cup – most children can drink from an open cup by 2 years of age.

What drinks should I put in their sippy cup?

Your baby’s sippy cup should only ever contain cooled boiled water, breastmilk or formula. It should never contain any other type of drink.

How can I get my baby to drink from a cup?

Practice, practice and more practice! Don’t worry, some babies take a while to get the hang of it, but they do get there.

Some tips to try:

  • showing your baby how to lift the cup to their mouth and tipping it up to drink
  • letting your baby see you (or another child) drink from a clean sippy cup – just make sure you always give your baby a clean cup – sharing cups without washing also shares bacteria from your mouth!
  • touching the tip of the spout to the roof of your baby’s mouth – this should stimulate their sucking reflex and show them that the spout is just like a nipple/teat
  • allowing them to play and experiment with the cup – it might make a mess but by tipping the cup upside down and seeing the water flow out, they’ll soon learn how it works!
  • making it a familiar part of meal time by always offering water in their sippy cup with each meal

As soon as your baby is ready, you can move onto an open cup – most children can drink from an open cup by 2 years of age.

Choking and gagging

Choking versus gagging

Babies often appear to cough and gag when they first try to swallow solid food – this is actually a good thing because the gag reflex is a safety mechanism that moves food from the back of the throat to the front, which helps prevent choking.

A baby who is gagging might make some noise and seems to be coughing mildly. As long as your baby is breathing fine and manages to spit out the food themselves, there is no need to worry or try to help.

A baby who is choking often can’t make much noise, if any at all. It is rare, but it doesn’t take long for a baby to choke (even on something small) and they may need your help. This is why it is so important to NEVER leave your baby alone while they are eating.

A good motto to remember is: loud and red (gagging) let them go ahead, silent and blue (choking) they need help from you.

Tips to avoid gagging and choking

Are they coughing or gagging?

Nutritional needs

Babies and children need a range of nutrients to grow and develop to their full potential, most of which come from their foods and drinks.

To make sure they get all these vital nutrients, it’s important to keep in mind that babies (and children) only have little tummies, which means they need to:

  • eat foods that are rich in nutrients – ’nutrient dense’ or everyday foods
  • avoid foods and drinks that fill them up quickly yet contain little nutrition – ’energy dense, nutrient poor’ or sometimes foods

Find out more about how you can promote healthy eating habits right from the start.

Iron

Iron is important for making sure your baby builds a healthy body and brain. Until around 6 months of age, your baby will get enough iron from breastmilk or formula but from 6 months, your baby needs more iron than your breastmilk or formula alone can provide.

At 6 months your baby's main food should still be breastmilk/formula but this is why it's important to start introducing solids which are high in iron such as iron fortified baby cereals, finely mashed meat, fish, chicken, legumes (such as baked beans or lentils) and leafy green vegetables.

Other nutritional needs

As long as your baby is being offered (and eating) a variety of different healthy foods and coloured fruit and vegetable purées as they start on solids, they should be getting all the nutrients they need.

Vitamin D, which is important for growing strong, healthy bones, is slightly different to the other nutrients. Although vitamin D can be found in oily fish and some fortified foods, the best source of vitamin D is the sun!

Your baby's skin actually makes vitamin D when in sunshine, which is a bonus because babies not only love being outdoors, but it also helps them to build healthy bones!

Just make sure your baby’s skin is not exposed to sunshine for more than a few minutes at a time, and never when UV levels are really high (such as in summer). This will help prevent burning, skin damage and later development of skin cancers.

Note: because babies’ skin is very sensitive and can burn easily, Cancer Council Australia recommends a combination of sun protection measures be used whenever UV Index levels reach three and above:

  • Slip on sun-protective clothing to cover as much skin as possible
  • Slop on SPF30+ sunscreen at least 20 minutes before going outside
  • Slap on a broad brim or legionnaire style hat
  • Seek shade
  • Slide on some sunglasses

Vitamin supplements

The breastmilk/formula and solids your baby eats should provide enough nutrients for your baby, so there is no need for any vitamins or supplements unless your GP or maternal and child health nurse recommends otherwise.

How to help your baby love vegetables!

Vegetables are essential for good health and should make up a large part of the diet – for both babies and grown-ups! They provide a range of different vitamins and antioxidants, as well as lots of fibre and water, which are vital to your baby’s growth and development, and help to protect your baby’s body against all kinds of diseases.

In order to eat healthily, we need to eat many vegetables, ideally including a rainbow of different colours, in addition to a wide range of foods from the other four main food groups.

The problem is that it often takes time for babies to learn to enjoy vegetables, largely because they tend to taste less sweet and more bitter than other foods. Bitter tastes can be associated with harmful substances such as poisons and toxins, so this initial rejection of bitter foods is thought to be an instinctive reaction to protect against harm.

The good news is that you can help your baby to learn to like them, in fact, starting early is the best way to help your baby have a life-long liking (and hopefully love) of vegetables!

Follow our top 10 tips to help your baby develop a love of vegetables!

1. Offer vegetables early and often
Babies who are offered vegetables every day, and from an early age, tend to continue this habit when they are older by eating a variety of different vegetables.

Along with high iron foods, make sure you offer your baby a variety of different coloured vegetables as their first solid foods. To help your baby learn to like the taste of different individual vegetables, try not to always mix or hide them in other foods.

2. Keep offering rejected vegetables – but avoid food battles!
Babies instinctively reject new foods – this is normal! In fact, it can take up to 10–15 tries of a new vegetable before your baby may learn to like it. The key is patience and persistence.

When offering your baby a new vegetable, try not to make a fuss about the vegetable or pressure or bribe them to eat it. If they refuse to eat it, just take away the uneaten vegetable without comment.

If they try the vegetable but then spit it out, don’t make a fuss, just take away the uneaten food without comment. Consider this a win though – it still counts as a try! Try offering that vegetable again tomorrow or another day or perhaps prepared in a different way.

3. Be a good role model – eat vegetables yourself!
Your baby will naturally copy what you do and eating is no exception! If your baby sees you enjoying your own vegetables, they are more likely to learn to enjoy the vegetables you offer them.

Tips to manage fussy eating

How you react to, and handle, food refusal is very important in avoiding meal time battles down the track!

Work out what could be causing the behaviour

Your baby might refuse to eat the food you’re offering for a number of reasons! Knowing what your baby’s signs are will help you to work out why they are being fussy.

Some reasons for being fussy can be because they are:

  • too full to eat
  • starting or helping with growing a vegetable patch
  • not hungry
  • tired
  • distracted
  • unwell
  • teething
  • not ready for solids yet.
  • just testing you out to see what they can get away with!

Try:

  • reducing (or stopping) your baby from ‘grazing’ between meals and snacks – grazing fills your baby up, often making them too full to try new foods at meal and snack times
  • only offering breastmilk/formula after eating solids once your baby has reached 8 months – this should make sure your baby hungry enough to try new foods

Don’t forget that it is normal for your baby’s appetite to change from day-to-day – they might eat lots one day and very little the next!

Don't give them alternative foods

When your baby refuses a meal/snack it can be tempting to give in and offer them something you know they will eat. The problem is that this only reinforces their behaviour as OK, teaching them to refuse more food in the future so they can get what they want.

Instead, just take the food away and move onto the next activity. Try not to offer a different food until the next planned meal or snack time.

Don’t worry, your baby will not starve themselves! They will eat when they are hungry and probably just eat more at the next meal or snack time if they need it. But if you are worried they won’t sleep well, you could also try offering a milk feed closer to bedtime to tide them over.

Keep trying!

It’s easy to fall into the trap of labelling a food refusal as a dislike of that food but just keep in mind that it can take up to 10–15 tries of a new food before your baby may accept it.

Try:

  • changing the way you talk about the food – your baby will pick up on how you talk about foods, especially as they get older and saying they “don’t like it” doesn’t help in your attempts to get them to try the food! As an example, instead of saying “Sienna doesn’t like green beans”, you might say “Sienna is still getting used to green beans”
  • not to fuss or give up, just try offering the food again another time, maybe even prepared in a different way

Try not to fuss, bribe or pressure your baby to eat

The more you fuss and try to control what your baby eats, the more they will fuss and continue to refuse the food. It becomes more about the power struggle than the food.

Bribing your baby to eat a food (e.g. “eat your vegetables and you can have dessert”) only ends up making the reward food (in this case, the dessert) something your baby really wants, and the food you’re trying to get them to eat (in this case, the vegetables) less desirable. It doesn’t help your baby learn to like new foods and if anything, only that they must ‘endure’ them to get what they really want.

Instead, if your baby has refused a food or meal, simply take it away, without comment or fuss, and remain calm. Don’t offer a different food. By not giving into their fussy eating now, you will be avoiding many food battles when they get older!

Limit the length of meal times

Keep your stress levels down by having meal time routine and not letting meal times drag on for ages.

Try to keep meal times to just 15–20 minutes. This helps your baby learn that when meal time is (and isn’t) and that food will not be offered outside of these times. Your baby will eat when they are hungry – they won’t starve themselves!

Be a healthy role model

One of the best ways to encourage your baby to eat healthy foods is by eating and enjoying them yourself!

And just because you don’t like a particular food or perhaps love another food, this doesn’t mean that your baby will be the same. Try to let your baby try a variety of healthy foods so they can work out what they like (and don’t like) themselves.

Only offer small amounts at a time

We might like to see our plates full, but babies and toddlers can feel a bit overwhelmed when they’re given large amounts of food.

Instead, it’s better to only offer small serves and give more later, if needed. This way, you shouldn’t end up wasting too much food (which can be frustrating!) or forcing your baby to finish all the food offered, which can lead to excess weight gain if they’re eating more than they feel they need.

Don’t forget, the amount of food your baby will eat will also change from day-to-day.

Introduce new foods with foods your baby already likes or knows

Babies are more likely to try a new food when they are served with something they like or are already familiar with.

If your baby initially refuses to try a new food, next time, try serving it with a food your baby is already familiar with or really likes.

Avoiding allergies

Preventing food allergies

To lower the risk of your baby developing allergies:

  • start introducing solids at around 6 months
  • offer your baby any new solids first thing in the morning so you can watch for any reactions over the day
  • continue breast/formula feeding your baby while introducing solids – milk is still their main meal and provides them with almost all the vital nutrients they need until 12 months
  • introduce foods commonly associated with allergies (such as cooked egg, nut spreads like peanut butter, dairy, fish and wheat) in the first 12 months – even if your baby has a high risk of developing food allergies

There is no need to introduce new foods slowly or one at a time – several can be introduced at once. Just try not to always serve new foods as part of a mixture, as this doesn’t let your baby try all the individual tastes and textures that foods can have.

Reactions

Babies can have different kinds of reactions to food, most of which are uncomfortable, but some can be life threatening if not treated immediately.

Some of the signs your baby may have had an allergic reaction or intolerance to something include:

  • skin rash/eczema
  • tummy upset (vomiting/diarrhoea)
  • unusual runny nose/eyes
  • unusual restlessness/crying

If you think your baby may have an allergy, or are not sure, stop that food and seek advice from your GP or maternal and child health nurse.

Serious food allergies (anaphylaxis)

Anaphylaxis is a severe allergic reaction that can be life-threatening if not treated right away. It can happen as quickly as just seconds or minutes after exposure to an allergen.

If your baby:

  • has difficulty breathing and/or swelling in the throat
  • develops skin redness/swelling

Poos and constipation

Has your baby's poo changed?

As you introduce your baby to a variety of new healthy foods you can expect your baby's poo to change in colour, smell and texture. Try not to get too worried about your baby's bowel habits – as long as your baby is gaining weight and appears happy, the poo changes are probably normal!

Constipation in babies

Constipation is when your baby's poo is hard and dry (and looks like marbles) – it is more common around the time solids are introduced. The number of poos a day can vary considerably from one baby to another; some babies have only one poo a week while others have several poos a day, but both are normal.

There are a couple of signs that your baby might be constipated:

  • they seem to be in pain or is distressed when pooing (a little straining no cause for concern)
  • you can’t flatten your baby's poo by squeezing it between the folds of the nappy

Preventing and easing constipation

Prevention is always better than cure, but the good news with constipation is that the following steps both help to ease and prevent it from happening in the first place.

To relieve/prevent your baby from being constipated:

  • introduce solids slowly – start with ½ a teaspoon a couple of times per day after their usual breastmilk or formula feed and increase gradually
  • avoid offering your baby lots of lentils and high fibre cereals such as oats – but do keep on providing a variety of healthy foods
  • if your baby is formula-fed, always make sure the formula is made up according to the manufacturer's instructions

        What to expect and tips

        What to expect

        When you start feeding both breastmilk and formula to your baby, you might notice a few changes.

        Your:

        • household costs will go up - you will be regularly purchasing tins of formula, and potentially other products you may not already have
        • routine will change - your baby may feed differently once they are also receiving formula
        • life might feel busier - it takes more time to sterilise and clean feeding equipment because formula is not a sterile product, so it's important to practise good hygiene

        Your breasts:

        • will produce less milk - regular feeding with formula can interfere with your milk supply
        • might start feeling too full and uncomfortable - make sure you check for blocked ducts and express to feel comfortable; your breasts will soon adjust to their new schedule

        Your baby:

        • might refuse to take the bottle - especially when mum is giving it to them
        • can start refusing your breast - milk comes out of a bottle far easier than from your breast (which needs lots of sucking), so your baby may protest
        • has changed poo consistency, smell and colour - just like adults, when your baby's diet changes, so too does their poo

        Remember:

        • if any changes you notice when mixed feeding concern you, speak with your GP or maternal and child health nurse
        • it is best to wait until your milk supply is established (around 6 weeks) before introducing any formula to your baby – even then, any regular formula feeds may still affect your milk supply
        • time, practice and patience are needed for changing you and your baby's feeding routine!

        Tips for mixed feeding

        1. Establish your milk supply before introducing a bottle

        Before introducing formula or a bottle, try to wait until your milk supply is established (about 6 weeks after your baby is born) so your milk supply is less affected and your baby is used to feeding from your breast.

        2. Take steps to keep your breastmilk supply up

        Any regular replacement of a breastfeed (even just once a day) will affect your milk supply because milk supply responds to the demand from your baby. One way to keep up your milk supply is to fit in some extra expression sessions.

        If you are going to 'top up' your baby with formula, be sure to:

        • always breastfeed first before offering the bottle
        • avoid giving your baby formula in between breastfeeds as this might make them too full to have a big breastfeed, which can then start a cycle of reducing demand on your milk supply, often referred to as the ‘top up trap’

        2. Let someone else give the bottle, if possible

        Some babies adjust to feeding from a bottle very quickly, while others need a bit of time. All babies are adaptable and can learn to feed from both the breast and from a bottle.

        While your baby is getting used to being fed from a bottle, it may be easier for somebody else to feed them. Your baby can smell your breastmilk and may refuse the bottle and/or formula if your breasts are close by!

        Once your baby is used to feeding from a bottle, use this time as important cuddling and bonding time as usual.

        Will formula help my baby sleep?

        Not necessarily – most babies normally wake through the night for the first few months.

        In fact, some research even shows that breastfeeding mums get more sleep, on average, than formula feeding mums because breastfeeding in the middle of the night is much quicker and easier than getting up to prepare a bottle!

        If you feel like you’re always tired, try these tips or perhaps consider:

        • sleeping in the same room as your baby so that you are close by
        • learning to breastfeed lying down so that you can keep on resting, even when feeding

        If your baby is older than 6 months, read more on helping them (and you!) sleep through the night.

        My baby cries and fusses on the breast – are they still hungry?

        The first year of life is full of constant changes for a baby! They are growing and developing all the time and can cry for lots of reasons – not just because they are hungry.

        I can't express much milk – does this mean I have a low milk supply?

        Not at all!

        Expressing is not a 'measure' of how much milk you make – your baby is much better at getting out your milk than your hand or a breast pump! Their sucking is also better at triggering your let-down reflex to get your milk flowing.

        You should be making enough milk if your baby:

        • has 5+ wet disposable nappies, or at least 6–8 pale, wet cloth/reusable nappies over 24 hours
        • has soft poos
        • has good skin tone and bright eyes
        • is generally content – even if sometimes fussy and unsettled
        • is gaining weight and growing in length and head circumference.

        Find out more about low milk supply or take our milk supply quiz.

        Will mixed feeding reduce my milk supply?

        It is likely that by regularly giving your baby formula, your milk supply will be affected. Your body responds to your baby's demand for milk, so if they are feeding less from the breast, then your body will make less milk. This is one of the reasons why it's important to be clear in your decision about why you want to mix feed and how you will do it.

        Remember, many mums worry they don't make enough milk for their baby, but this is unlikely if you have been feeding your baby whenever they are hungry and they are attached on properly. If your baby is unsettled it may not be because they're hungry!

        How much formula should I give my baby?

        The amount of formula you give your baby will depend on your reasons for giving it. Remember, any regular replacement of a breastmilk will lead to a reduction in your milk supply (your body gets used to making less breastmilk because your baby is removing less).

        If you are given specific advice from a health professional on how much formula to give – you should follow that advice.

        If you want to keep up your milk supply while also giving formula, be sure to:

        • always breastfeed before offering the bottle
        • avoid giving your baby formula between breastfeeds as this might make them too full to have a big breastfeed which can then start a cycle of reducing demand on your milk supply
        • start slowly and with small amounts of formula – the average 4kg baby that is being exclusively formula-fed will only have around 100mL at each formula feed, so start with much less than this
        • express your milk and/or give extra breastfeeds when you can
        • be guided by your baby's appetite – don't force them to finish a bottle

        Important tips

        What texture is right for my baby?
        Just as a variety of different foods is important, so is a variety of age-appropriate textures. Varying the food texture helps your baby learn to chew (even if they don't yet have teeth!) as well as swallow.
        Generally, if your baby is

        • 6 - 8 months - use only puréed and mashed foods but move to minced (or mashed with a fork) and chopped foods soon
        • 8 months - begin finger foods.
        • 12 months - your baby can eat the variety of foods eaten by the rest of the family

        See the video library for some tips on preparing different textures, including how to make some adult meals age appropriate for your baby.

        Keep the cost down

        • fresh fruit and vegetables that are 'in season' are cheaper, tastier and more nutritious
        • if you don't have one of the ingredients listed in a recipe, try substituting ingredients or experimenting, rather than specifically buying the item

        Keep it healthy

        • avoid adding sugar, salt (including stocks), extra fats and oils to foods for your baby
        • choose tinned fruit that is ‘unsweetened’ or 'in fruit juice' instead of syrup
        • practice good hygiene by washing hands, using clean utensils and working on a clean counter
        • It is OK to use a little cow's milk in cooking before your baby is 12 months old

        • for the most part, your baby can eat the same meals as you, meaning you don't need to prepare separate meals - look for those recipes labelled as good for the whole family and follow the instructions at the end to adapt the recipe 'to serve the family'
        • cook up more than you need and keep extras in the fridge or freeze extra for quick easy meals at a later time
        • keep some frozen vegetables in the freezer and tinned fruit in the cupboard as back-ups for when you run out of fresh vegetables and fruit

        Milk

        The type of milk your baby should have depends on their age. Breastmilk or formula provides all the nutrition your baby needs up to around 6 months. The following guidelines should be followed unless your GP or maternal and child health nurse advises you otherwise:

        • from 8 months, offer your baby breastmilk/formula after solids meals to make sure they are hungry enough to eat the new iron rich foods they now need
        • from 6 months, if you are formula feeding, you may wish to offer formula in a sippy cup rather than a bottle.
        • cow's milk should not be given as a drink until your baby is older than 12 months but small quantities used in cooking are OK
        • until your baby is 2 years or older, always provide regular full cream cow's milk (instead of low/reduced fat cow's milk as this doesn't contain enough energy for your growing baby)

        Water

        • If your baby is under 6 months old, they will get all the hydration they need from breastmilk/formula (even in hot weather) and should not be given water.
        • From 6 months it is OK to give your baby small sips of cooled boiled tap water if they seem thirsty (e.g. in hot weather) but this shouldn't be done regularly as it can fill your baby's little tummy, making them feel too full to drink enough milk to get the energy they need to grow.
        • It's a good idea to encourage your baby to drink (milk, or water if having) from a cup after 6 months of age!

        Fruit juice

        Fruit juice should not form part of your baby's diet for a few reasons:

        • compared with the whole fruit, it's far easier to drink too much which can cause your baby to gain too much weight
          • it takes about 3 whole oranges to make just 1 cup of orange juice
          • there is no chewing involved, so it can be swallowed quickly
          • most of the fibre is lost so it passes through more quickly
        • too much can cause diarrhoea
        • the natural sugars can cause tooth decay - even before the teeth have come through

        Cordial / fizzy drinks / soft-drinks / energy drinks

        Cordials, fizzy drinks, soft-drinks and energy drinks should never be given to your baby as they can damage growing teeth and cause your baby to gain too much weight. Energy drinks also contain high levels of caffeine which can be harmful to your baby's developing body.
