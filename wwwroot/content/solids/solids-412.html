Starting solids

How do I get started?

  • when to start

  • how much to offer

You might also find these tips for getting started helpful!

When to start

It is recommended that you wait until 6 months before introducing solid foods to your baby's diet. This gives your baby's body time to develop to a stage where it is ready to manage solids and ensures that they are still getting all the nutrition they need from breastmilk/formula.

Your baby should start showing signs of being ready for solids around 6 months but you can also to check if your baby is ready.

Your baby's first solid foods should be high in iron. This is because, from 6 months, your baby's iron needs are greater than what your breastmilk or formula can provide.

What foods to try (and avoid)

The order or number of foods introduced at a time doesn't really matter, just as long as your baby is getting iron rich foods that are mashed/puréed down to a suitable texture for their age.

It won't be long before your baby will be eating much the same foods as the rest of the family, just at a texture that suits them!

Foods to try
Iron rich foods that make great first foods include:

  • iron fortified baby cereals
  • meat
  • fish
  • chicken
  • legumes (such as baked beans or lentils)
  • leafy green vegetables

Foods to add later:

  • vegetables
  • fruits
  • cheese
  • full fat unsweetened plain natural yoghurt

To train your baby's immune system, it is important to give them a variety of different foods within their first year, except those on the avoid list.

You should even give your baby foods that often considered as allergenic, such as:

  • peanut butter
  • cooked egg
  • dairy
  • wheat products

This is true, even if your baby is at high risk of allergy. Just keep an eye out for any reactions (such as a rash or upset tummy) and if you do notice one, or are not sure, stop that food and contact your GP. Learn more about allergies and how to avoid them.

What to avoid in the first 12 months
Choking hazards:

  • whole nuts
  • raw foods
  • hard foods

Foods your baby's body is not ready for:

  • honey - this can cause botulism (a serious bacterial infection) in babies under 12 months
  • cow's and goat's milks (except in small quantities in cooking)
  • additions such as sugar, salt (including stocks), extra fats and oils

Treat or sometimes foods which can fill your baby up without providing much (or any) nutritional value, leaving little room for the nutritious foods your baby needs for optimal growth and development. Avoid giving your baby a taste for these foods (and making it harder to encourage healthy eating habits later on) by not offering:

  • chocolate
  • chips
  • biscuits
  • cakes
  • ice cream
  • lollies

Until 12 months, the only drinks your baby should be having are breastmilk or formula and small amounts of cooled boiled water; avoid giving your baby other drinks such as:

  • juices (including 100% fruit juice)
  • soft drinks
  • cordials
  • sports drinks
  • tea (including herbal)
  • coffee

How, when and what textures

How and when
The first thing to keep in mind is that you provide the food to your baby and your baby decides if they will eat it and if so, how much. A good motto to remember is: parents provide, babies decide.

Just like the rest of us, all babies are different, so some will enjoy solids faster than others will. At first, your baby will probably end up spitting most foods out!

Although messy, letting your baby explore their food with their fingers (including purées) allows them to smell, feel and hopefully even taste new foods - it's all part of the learning experience!

To help you get started:

  • Until at least 8 months, start with offering solids twice a day after their normal breastmilk/formula feed so they still get the nutrients they need from the milk before filling up on solids.
  • Sit at the same level as your baby and try not to fuss - if they spit out or refuse to accept the food, just try again another time.
  • Offer solids when both you and your baby are happy and relaxed.
  • Allow your baby to sit with you when you are eating or at family meal times, such as breakfast.
  • Over the first few weeks, gradually increase how often you offer solids.

Textures
Just as a variety of different foods is important, so is a variety of age-appropriate textures. In the past, most babies had to stick to puréed foods for a longer time because they started solids before they were ready but when babies start solids at around 6 months, they can begin learning about other textures fairly quickly. Varying the food texture helps your baby learn to chew (even without teeth!) as well as to swallow.

Start with puréed or smooth food and gradually progress to lumpier foods as soon as your baby tolerates it. These lumpier foods will help teach your baby how to chew their food (no teeth required) which then helps them to speak.

Generally, if your baby is:

  • 6-8 months - use only puréed and mashed foods but move to minced (or mashed with a fork) and chopped foods soon
  • 8 months - begin finger foods
  • 12 months - your baby might even be ready to start eating foods with the same textures enjoyed by the rest of the family

Just make sure you introduce lumpy food before your baby is 10 months of age to avoid feeding difficulties down the track, such as only accepting smooth or soft foods.

How much solid food should you offer?

At first, your baby will probably only eat very small amounts. Learning how to swallow new textures is just like any other new skill - it takes time to master!

Try:

  • starting with a ½ teaspoon twice a day, until your baby is used to the different texture
  • then gradually increasing to a few tablespoons per feed over the next few weeks
  • giving your baby some finger foods - soft pieces of food that your baby can hold and try feeding to themselves

As long as you provide your baby with healthy solid food options, they will decide if, and how much of it, they will eat.

Milk or solids first?

  • Until your baby is at least 8 months old it's important that they still have their normal breastmilk/formula feed before any solids, to ensure they're still getting all the nutrients they need.
  • After 8 months offer your baby breastmilk/formula after solid meals to make sure they are hungry enough to eat the new iron rich foods they now need.

If you are worried that your baby's growth is not tracking well speak to your GP or your maternal and child health nurse.

Signs of being hungry for solids (and full)

Signs that your baby is hungry for solids include:

  • getting excited when they see you getting their food ready
  • leaning towards you while they are sitting in the highchair
  • opening their mouth as you're about to feed them

From 7-8 months, your baby may reach for the spoon or for food when they are hungry, or point to food.

From 8-12 months, your baby may also show excitement when you give them food.

Signs that your baby is no longer hungry include:

  • refusing to eat or closing their mouth tightly
  • pushing away the spoon, or your hand
  • turning their head away from you
  • looking upset or starting to cry

If your baby shows these signs, it's important to follow their cues and not pressure them to eat or complete the meal. This encourages them to trust their own appetite and not to overeat when they are older.

How to deal with food being spat out

Just because your baby spits out new foods, it doesn't always mean they don't like them - they just need time to get used to them! In fact, it can take up to 10-15 tries of a new food before your baby may accept it - so try not to give up!

It is normal for babies to spit out new and unknown foods, especially vegetables, which tend to be less sweet and more bitter than other foods. It's thought that because bitter tastes can be associated with poisons/toxins, this initial rejection of bitter foods is an instinctive reaction to protect against harm.

If your baby spits out a food, the best thing to do is to try to react calmly and take the food away, before moving onto the next activity.

Resist the temptation to offer a different food as this can lead to food battles and fussy eating down the track!

As frustrating as spat out food can be, try to keep in mind that your baby needs time to explore the new texture/taste and they will take more in at each feed as they grow and get more used to solid food.
