Nutritional needs

Babies and children need a range of nutrients to grow and develop to their full potential, most of which come from their foods and drinks.

To make sure they get all these vital nutrients, it's important to keep in mind that babies (and children) only have little tummies, which means they need to:

  • eat foods that are rich in nutrients - 'nutrient dense' or everyday foods
  • avoid foods and drinks that fill them up quickly yet contain little nutrition - 'energy dense, nutrient poor' or sometimes foods

Find out more about how you can promote healthy eating habits right from the start.

Iron

Iron is important for making sure your baby builds a healthy body and brain. Until around 6 months of age, your baby will get enough iron from breastmilk or formula but from 6 months, your baby needs more iron than your breastmilk or formula alone can provide.

At 6 months your baby's main food should still be breastmilk/formula but this is why it's important to start introducing solids which are high in iron such as iron fortified baby cereals, finely mashed meat, fish, chicken, legumes (such as baked beans or lentils) and leafy green vegetables.

Other nutritional needs

As long as your baby is being offered (and eating) a variety of different healthy foods and coloured fruit and vegetable purees as they start on solids, they should be getting all the nutrients they need.

Vitamin D, which is important for growing strong, healthy bones, is slightly different to the other nutrients. Although vitamin D can be found in oily fish and some fortified foods, the best source of vitamin D is the sun!

Your baby's skin actually makes vitamin D when in sunshine, which is a bonus because babies not only love being outdoors, but it also helps them to build healthy bones!

Just make sure your baby's skin is not exposed to sunshine for more than a few minutes at a time, and never when UV levels are really high (such as in summer). This will help prevent burning, skin damage and later development of skin cancers.

Note: because babies' skin is very sensitive and can burn easily, Cancer Council Australia recommends a combination of sun protection measures be used whenever UV Index levels reach three and above:

  • Slip on sun-protective clothing to cover as much skin as possible
  • Slop on SPF30+ sunscreen at least 20 minutes before going outside
  • Slap on a broad brim or legionnaire style hat
  • Seek shade
  • Slide on some sunglasses

Vitamin supplements

The breastmilk/formula and solids your baby eats should provide enough nutrients for your baby, so there is no need for any vitamins or supplements unless your GP or maternal and child health nurse recommends otherwise.
