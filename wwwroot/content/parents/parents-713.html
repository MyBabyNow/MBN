Sleep

Benefits of sleep
One of the biggest challenges of being a new parent is getting enough sleep! Adults generally need 7-8 hours of sleep per night but as a new parent, your baby crying and needing feeds throughout the night can make this near impossible!

It can be tempting to accept feeling tired as normal and to 'just get on with it' but getting enough sleep is just as important for you as it is for your baby.

Some signs that you might need more sleep include:

  • difficulty concentrating
  • feeling down or being moody
  • feeling anxious
  • problems with coordination or being more clumsy than normal

Getting enough sleep can help you feel happier and calmer. It can also help you more fully enjoy the experience of being a new parent, which in turn will help your baby feel more relaxed.

Not getting enough sleep also adds up, so if you feel tired or notice any of the above signs, try to prioritise getting more sleep.

Try these tips for sneaking in more sleep now you're a new parent.

Sleep tips
Although your old sleeping patterns may no longer be possible, there are a number of ways to make sure you still get enough sleep. It's even possible to manage enough sleep when you're breastfeeding!

Take naps
Napping can be a great way to increase the amount of sleep you get, especially during early weeks and months when you may be waking often at night to feed your baby.

A nap should last no longer than 15-30 minutes. Any longer and your body may think it has had enough sleep and stop you from sleeping well at night.

Not sure where to start?

Try:

  • lying down for a rest, even if you find it hard to sleep during the day - this still helps to recharge the batteries
  • having a nap or a rest when your baby sleeps
  • setting an alarm to make sure you don't nap for longer than 30 minutes
  • to nap or rest at the same time each day if possible

Cut down on caffeine
Caffeine can make you feel good in the short term but can make sleeping and resting harder. Keep in mind that if you're breastfeeding, any caffeine you consume also passes into your breastmilk and can upset your baby.

Because caffeine stays in your system for more than 6 hours, it's best to avoid heavily caffeinated drinks (such as coffee, colas and tea) late in the afternoon and evening when they are more likely to interfere with your sleep patterns.

Not sure how much caffeine is hiding in that drink? Check out our caffeine guide.

Share the load
You don't have to do it all! Having someone look after your baby while you nap or take time out can work wonders.
Try:

  • sharing the work of looking after your baby with your partner, or ask family or friends for help
  • taking turns for night time duties with your partner (if you're breastfeeding, your partner could do the nappy changing and re-settling following a feed so you can get back to sleep quicker - see other ways your partner can help you with breastfeeding).

Have your own space to sleep
Mums in particular might find it helpful to have their own space to nap. This is best in a dark and quiet room, away from your partner and/or baby, to reduce the chance of being disturbed.

Avoid screens before bedtime
It's tempting to relax in front of the TV, computer or smartphone at night, but try to turn it off at least an hour before you go to bed. Lights from screens have been shown to disrupt sleep.
