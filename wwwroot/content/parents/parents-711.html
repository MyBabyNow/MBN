Childbirth recovery

Creating and giving birth to a baby takes its toll on a woman's body! And although you might wish to just get back to normal as much as possible, there are a couple of things that you should be aware of following childbirth, including when it's likely safe for mum to start getting physically active again.

Strengthening your pelvic floor

Your pelvic floor is a set of muscles that hold your bladder, uterus andbowel in place and give you control over your bladder and bowel. Although pregnancy and childbirth can weaken these muscles, you can help your pelvic floor recover by regularly doing the exercises that your midwife gave you during pregnancy.

Unless you've been told otherwise, you can start doing pelvic floor strengthening exercises immediately after you've given birth -learn how to do them and ways to make them just another part of your day.

Around 6 weeks after childbirth, your body will have more than likelyreturned to normal. If you are worried about your body or any changes you have noticed, it is best to speak to your GP or maternal and child health nurse.

Postpartum haemorrhoids (piles)

Postpartum haemorrhoids or 'piles' are common, especially when you've had a baby, but can be prevented by eating a healthy diet high in fibre to avoid constipation.

High fibre foods to help prevent constipation include:

  • fruits and vegetables (with skins on)
  • wholegrain foods (e.g. breads and cereals, brown rice)
  • nuts, seeds and grains (e.g. almonds, linseeds, sunflower seeds)
  • bran (e.g. wheat bran or rice bran)
  • dried beans

It's also important to stay hydrated to avoid constipation when you eat additional fibre. Be sure to drink enough fluid - ideally water - try to reach around 8 glasses a day (9 if you're pregnant or breastfeeding). Read more about eating healthily.

Physical activity

Moderate or vigorous intensity physical activity will not affect your ability to breastfeed successfully but make sure you drink plenty of fluids, especially water, to avoid dehydration..

If you had a caesarean birth, it is best to avoid vigorous physical activity (e.g. running) for at least 6 weeks so you can heal properly. If you are worried about this or have questions, make sure you talk with your GP or maternal and child health nurse.

Some tips:

  • wear a good sports bra for physical activity of a moderate to vigorous intensity (e.g. when you will be puffing hard)
  • high impact physical activity (e.g. running or a gym class) is best done after breastfeeding because your breasts will be relatively empty (less full of breastmilk) which will reduce bounce and discomfort.
