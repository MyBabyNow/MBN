Mixed feeding

What to expect

When you start feeding both breastmilk and formula to your baby, you might notice a few changes.

Your:

  • household costs will go up - you will be regularly purchasing tins of formula, and potentially other products you may not already have
  • routine will change - your baby may feed differently once they are also receiving formula
  • life might feel busier - it takes more time to sterilise and clean feeding equipment because formula is not a sterile product, so it's important to practise good hygiene

Your breasts:

  • will produce less milk - regular feeding with formula can interfere with your milk supply
  • might start feeling too full and uncomfortable - make sure you check for blocked ducts and express to feel comfortable; your breasts will soon adjust to their new schedule

Your baby:

  • might refuse to take the bottle - especially when mum is giving it to them
  • can start refusing your breast - milk comes out of a bottle far easier than from your breast (which needs lots of sucking), so your baby may protest
  • has changed poo consistency, smell and colour - just like adults, when your baby's diet changes, so too does their poo

Remember:

  • if any changes you notice when mixed feeding concern you, speak with your GP or maternal and child health nurse
  • it is best to wait until your milk supply is established (around 6 weeks) before introducing any formula to your baby - even then, any regular formula feeds may still affect your milk supply
  • time, practice and patience are needed for changing you and your baby's feeding routine!

Tips for mixed feeding

1. Establish your milk supply before introducing a bottle
Before introducing formula or a bottle, try to wait until your milk supply is established (about 6 weeks after your baby is born) so your milk supply is less affected and your baby is used to feeding from your breast.

2. Take steps to keep your breastmilk supply up
Any regular replacement of a breastfeed (even just once a day) will affect your milk supply because milk supply responds to the demand from your baby. One way to keep up your milk supply is to fit in some extra expression sessions.

If you are going to 'top up' your baby with formula, be sure to:

  • always breastfeed first before offering the bottle
  • avoid giving your baby formula in between breastfeeds as this might make them too full to have a big breastfeed, which can then start a cycle of reducing demand on your milk supply, often referred to as the 'top up trap'

3. Let someone else give the bottle, if possible
Some babies adjust to feeding from a bottle very quickly, while others need a bit of time. All babies are adaptable and can learn to feed from both the breast and from a bottle.

While your baby is getting used to being fed from a bottle, it may be easier for somebody else to feed them. Your baby can smell your breastmilk and may refuse the bottle and/or formula if your breasts are close by!

Once your baby is used to feeding from a bottle, use this time as important cuddling and bonding time as usual.
