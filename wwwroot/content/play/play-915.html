0-1 month

  • Development at 0-1 month
  • Play ideas

How is your newborn developing in their first month?
Cuddling, sleeping, feeding and tummy time - that's what it's all about in the first few months!

TYour newborn is still getting used to being in the outside world and the free movement that goes with it. It will be a few months before they will be able to roll over or sit up, even with support, but regular tummy time will help them build the strength their head, neck and upper body will needs when the time comes.

Your baby is also learning a lot as you spend time together every day. The sounds and sights of the world around your baby, as well as everything they can touch, is helping their brain to grow and develop.

What you might notice:

  • they lock their eyes onto your face and follow it - your face is the most interesting thing to your baby around this age!
  • most of the time your baby's hands will be in tight fists but sometimes they'll hold your finger
  • during tummy time, your baby may briefly lift their head (or turn it to the side if laying on their back) to see more of what's going on around them and/or where you are
  • objects and images of high contrast, such as things that are black and white or red, blue and yellow are of interest, as well as toys with faces or patterns like swirls or checks
  • they can hear you and know the sound of your voice, but can also startle at times when you speak or they hear some other sound
  • crying is the most common way your baby communicates with you (sometimes it might just be throaty noises), although they'll probably also make eye contact with you to let you know they want your attention

Developmental concerns
All babies develop in the same order, just at different rates. If you're worried about your baby's development, it's a good idea to speak with your GP or maternal and child health nurse.

Playing with your newborn forms an important part of their development but it's also a great way to interact and bond with them - all you need to get started is you and your baby!

Tummy time
Tummy time simply refers to placing your baby on their tummy when they're awake. It can be done in many different ways, including both indoors or outside, and should take place several times every day, from soon after birth. Over time, a good goal to aim for is 30 minutes of tummy time every day, spread throughout the whole day (it doesn't have to be 30 minutes all in one go).

Why do it?
Tummy time forms a vital part of a baby's development, eventually helping them build the strength to walk. Find out more about why tummy time is so important.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • familiar blanket (such as one mum has slept with as this scent might help relax your baby) or a clean and safe surface
  • toys/books/music etc (optional)
  • rolled-up towel (optional)
  • big exercise ball (optional)

How?

  • choose a calm environment
  • lay down the blanket or choose a clean and safe surface
  • place your baby on their tummy
  • entertain (or distract!) your baby to increase the amount of time they spend on their tummy - check out these ideas for entertaining/distracting them
  • start with trying 1-2 minutes at a time - even 30 seconds to start with is worthwhile!
  • build up to 30 minutes every day, spread throughout the whole day
  • once your baby is used to tummy time and a bit older, you could try tummy time on a big exercise ball - this makes tummy time fun and very interactive, as you will need to support your baby as they roll and bounce gently on the ball!

Don't worry if your baby doesn't take to tummy time straight away - some babies need to practise it many times before they actually enjoy tummy time!

If entertaining/distracting them doesn't seem to work, these ideas might help your baby get used to tummy time.

What is important is that you keep trying!

Skin-to-skin bonding
Skin-to-skin bonding simply means holding your newborn against the bare chest of a parent or carer. It is also an easy and fun way for partners to get involved in caring for their newborn.

Why do it?
Research has shown skin-to-skin contact has many benefits including increased cognitive function and assists in both bonding and attachment. It's also a great way to get in some tummy time.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • blanket (optional)

How?

  • with your baby either naked or in a nappy, remove your clothes from your top half or unbutton your shirt to reveal your bare chest
  • sit down and get comfy - relaxing while slightly leaning back is perfect safe surface
  • lay your baby tummy down on your bare chest and relax
  • if needed, cover yourselves with a blanket to keep warm

If you do this by leaning back, it also helps your baby get more comfortable with lying on their tummy for play time, but don't cput them on their back if they fall asleep.

Get out and about
There are so many reasons to spend time outside with your baby - whether that be going for a stroll, heading to a playground or just heading to the shops! It's also an easy way for partners to get involved in looking after their baby.

Why spend time outdoors?
Being outside is great for your baby's learning due to all the different sensations and noises!

Time spent outside means you both benefit from some fresh air and the chance to get some vitamin D from the sunshine. Your own mood and energy levels, which can be low after having a baby, should also get a boost.

Being outdoors lets your baby experience the social elements of the outside world, such as smiling passersby and shopkeepers. These interactions let your baby experience different speech patterns while also teaching them how to interact with other people.

Don't let a less-than-perfect day stop you from heading outside - babies can learn from all sorts of climates - rain, snow or sunshine!

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • appropriate clothing
  • sun protection if necessary (see note below)
  • stroller / baby carrier

How?

  • dress appropriately for the weather (including sun protection if required)
  • put your baby into the stroller or baby carrier
  • head outside to enjoy the different seasons - the changing colours of the trees, the birds, flowers and even the temperature differences!

Try to get into the habit of playing outside with your baby from an early age to help establish enjoying being outdoors as part of everyday life as your baby grows up!

Note: because babies' skin is very sensitive and can burn easily, Cancer Council Australia recommends a combination of sun protection measures be used whenever UV Index levels reach three and above:

  • Slip on sun-protective clothing to cover as much skin as possible
  • Slop on SPF30+ sunscreen at least 20 minutes before going outside
  • Slap on a broad brim or legionnaire style hat
  • Seek shade
  • Slide on some sunglasses

Read a black, white and red book
Reading a book with your baby is a good way to start practising sharing in activities together which will be important throughout life. It's also a great one-on-one activity and an easy way for partners and other carers to get involved.

Isn't it a bit early?
Your baby is never too young to have a book read to them! In fact, research shows that children who were read to as newborns have larger vocabularies and more advanced maths skills.

Reading to your baby exposes them to visuals such as letters, shapes and colours (which they will soon start to recognise) and helps them to start understanding emotions through the different characters in the books.

Why black, white and red books?
Your baby's sight is still developing when they are first born - they are still learning to focus on things and can't see colours straight away.

Because they can really only make out shapes where dark and light meet, books with images that have high contrast, such as those in black, white, grey and maybe even some red, are perfect for reading to your baby over the first couple of weeks.

Young babies can only focus on things that are about 20-30cm away - about the distance from your breast to your face (which is why it's often called the 'cuddle distance'), so make sure you hold the book close too!

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • black/white/grey/red baby book

How?

  • get yourself and your baby comfortable - this could be on the lounge, at the kitchen table, in a park or just before bedtime.
  • hold the book no further than 20-30cm away from your baby's face
  • get your baby involved by over-exaggerating the words/the characters and pointing out all the pictures

What's happening with your baby at 1-2 months?
Your baby is getting better at moving! They might have started lifting their head up and turning it from side to side during tummy time and might even be able to lift up their chest! This is a good sign that they are strengthening their head, neck and upper body in preparation for rolling over and eventually sitting up.

Around this time, most babies tend to cry and fuss more - this is a normal part of development and will pass in time. Every baby is different, but crying and fussing usually peaks around 6-8 weeks and starts to settle at around 12-16 weeks.

What you might also notice:

  • they've discovered they have fingers and hands - their hands will probably be open half the time now and they will grab onto a rattle when you put it in their palm but they may also hold both hands together
  • your baby has already made a strong bond with you, recognising you and responding to your voice and smile; from about 6 weeks, your baby might have even started smiling as well!
  • that you're being watched as you move around - that's because your baby can see a lot more now than a month ago and will follow you with their eyes, both from side to side and up and down
  • when it's time for a feed, your baby might open their mouth when they see your breast or a bottle
  • your baby's hearing is improving - they will look at you when you speak to them
  • they're also getting more vocal, so you might hear them coo and make single vowel sounds like 'a' or 'o'

Developmental concerns
All babies develop in the same order, just at different rates. If you're worried about your baby's development, it's a good idea to speak with your GP or maternal and child health nurse.

Sled & slide
Your baby isn't able to move around just yet, but they will soon be up and crawling!

Sliding your baby gently on a blanket sled is a simple way to prepare your baby for the forward motion of crawling and can be lots of fun for both of you.

Why do it?
The slow movement of the blanket will give the sensation of crawling and help your baby to develop spatial awareness.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • safe open space - carpet or smooth flooring surface (but be extra careful on slippery wooden floors)
  • blanket

How?

  • choose a safe open space
  • lay down the blanket
  • place your baby on their tummy with their arms out (or down beside them if they can't yet hold their head up)
  • slowly and gently slide the blanket towards you, making sure you maintain eye contact and keep talking to your baby

Remember to be gentle when sliding the blanket as sudden movements may startle your baby!

Outdoor tummy play
Outdoor tummy play simply refers to tummy time, but done outside! In fact, tummy time is a great activity to do anywhere and anytime (except when sleeping).

Most people think of tummy time as an indoor activity but your baby might actually find the outdoor environment more stimulating, which means they will be happy to stay on their tummy a little longer. Try to aim for a total of 30 minutes of tummy time every day, spread throughout the whole day.

Why do it?
Tummy time forms a vital part of a baby's development, eventually helping them build the strength to move independently. Find out more about why tummy time is so important.

What you need

  • sun protection if necessary

Don't stress if your baby doesn't take to tummy time straight away - some babies need to practise it many times before they actually enjoy tummy time!

Exploring the sense of smell
Babies are born with a great sense of smell, so letting them experience lots of (non-overpowering) scents to see how they react, can be fun for both of you!

Why do it?
Your baby's sense of smell is the most advanced sense they have at birth. It is initially used to stay close to their mother and to find breastmilk.

Smell eventually becomes closely linked to food preferences, so by exposing your baby to lots of different smells over time, you are likely helping them to accept a wide variety of different foods later on.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • lightly scented items, such as:
    • flowers (e.g. roses, jasmine or citrus flowers)
    • everyday foods (e.g. spices, lemon, vanilla, banana, strawberries or slices of orange)

How?

  • choose a calm environment
  • hold up a lightly scented item, just under your baby's nose
  • watch how your baby reacts - are they:
    • startled?
    • squinting or eyes wide open?
    • smiling?

Keep repeating the same smells many times with your baby over the next few months. Just like with foods when introducing solids, it can take many tries before a baby can work out if they like or dislike something!

Communicate with your baby
Encouraging your baby to communicate using noises, rhythms, or even facial expressions is fun and helps them learn to communicate!

Why do it?
Your baby is learning about language right from the start! The noises they are starting to make is their attempt at communication. The more responsive you are to these attempts, the more they will try to carry on a conversation.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby

How?

  • get into a position where you can sit face-to-face with your baby (about 30cm from their face so they can focus on it)
  • imitate the sounds your baby makes - they are likely to start making these noises on purpose as you converse
  • make extra sounds for your baby, then wait to see if your baby mimics any of them - use your imagination and have fun!
    • try to keep your noises short and rhythmic
    • sounds with big mouth movements make it easier for your baby to see how the noise was made (e.g. 'la la la', 'ba ba ba' or clicking your tongue)
  • now try different facial expressions - happy, surprised, bored and sticking your tongue out - hold each expression then pause to see if your baby starts mimicking an expression or if they stick their tongue out at you
  • sing songs with your baby while moving their body along to the actions in the lyrics, for example:
    • Row, row, row your boat - arms rowing on the sides
    • If you're happy and you know it, clap your hands - hands clapping

What's happening with your baby at 2-3 months?
Your baby is two months old, going on three already - where does the time go?!

They may or may not have started showing signs of rolling over and even though it's still quite some time before your baby will stand and walk, you might have noticed their legs are getting more active! They might be stretched out during tummy time or when laying on their back and if you hold your baby upright in a standing position (e.g. on the floor or your lap) they might even try standing!

Although your baby might still be crying and fussing, they are also becoming more aware of their surroundings and experiencing rapid development.

You might also notice your baby:

  • rest on their forearms or roll onto their side during tummy time
  • bring their hands together sometimes, and their hands are probably open most of the time now (except for when they're opening and closing them all the time!) - they might also be very curious about their fingers!
  • begin to use their eyes and hands together, perhaps even reaching for your face or swinging their hands towards an object
  • understanding that voices and faces go together (especially yours!) and because of the strong attachment they have made with you, they might follow you with their eyes, enjoy smiling at you and possibly even echo back at you when you speak to them
  • showing emotions like interest, disgust, distress and enjoyment
  • being more interested in different objects, like small blocks and toys, and their eyes can now follow objects moving in a circle
  • is communicating in different ways - hungry cries might be clearly different from cries of pain for example, and although they'll still coo (and might even start laughing!) they'll likely also use their face and body to try to tell you things

Swatting at objects
Your baby can't yet sit up by themselves but they are ready to swat at objects above them while on their back or out in front during tummy time! It's also lots of fun for your baby, especially when they actually make contact with objects and are praised for their achievement!

Why swat at hanging objects?
Reaching for and swatting at objects is fun and helps to develop their hand and eye coordination. It's also a good way to distract your baby during tummy time, which helps them spend longer doing it.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • string
  • fun objects that are baby safe, have a hole for easy attachment and might be of interest to your baby (e.g. rattles, plastic measuring spoons, laminated pictures etc)
  • branch
  • blanket / pram
  • sun protection if necessary
  • play mat with over-hanging objects (optional)

How?

  • dress appropriately for the weather (including sun protection if required)
  • find a strong branch to hang objects from
  • tie one or many of the objects to the branch so they dangle within swatting range but just out of grabbing range
  • place your baby underneath the objects on their back either in their pram or laying on their back on a blanket
    or
    lay your baby on their tummy with the objects hanging in front of them
  • encourage your baby to reach out and swat the objects - and don't forget to cheer when they make contact with them!

If you would rather not make your own, play mats with over-hanging objects can be purchased from baby shops or you could just dangle the objects in front of your baby.

Note: never let your baby play this game on their own or leave them alone with the string.

Rolling over
Tummy time helps your baby build the strength needed for crawling and eventually walking, but rolling over also plays an important role. Even if your baby can't yet do it by themselves, you can help and show your baby how to roll over.

Why encourage rolling over?
Rolling over helps to further develop your baby's muscle strength, balance and eye strength.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • familiar blanket or a clean and safe surface
  • favourite/interesting object (optional)

How?

  • choose a calm environment
  • lay down the blanket or choose a clean and safe surface
  • place your baby on their back
  • gently lift a leg (not too high) while holding down the other leg
  • using the lifted leg, gently roll them over to their tummy
  • encourage your baby to roll by themselves by placing a favourite or interesting object just out of reach and then encourage them to reach for it with their opposing hand (i.e. across their body)

Make sure you help and/or encourage them to roll both ways - this helps them build up strength on both sides of their body.

Massage your baby
Lightly massaging your baby is a great way for you to teach your baby about all their body parts e.g. 'this is your hand, these are your fingers, look at your little nails'. It can be soothing for your baby and also promotes bonding through touch and voice.

Why massage your baby?
Babies are developing a sense of identity. Massage helps babies increase their understanding of their identity by learning where they start and end - you are you, I am me, and we are separate. It takes about three years to complete this process.

What you need

  • parent or carer (with short fingernails!)
  • your baby
  • towel or safe and comfortable surface
  • moisturiser or baby massage oil (optional)

How?

  • place your baby on their back, without clothes if using oil/moisturiser
  • talk to your baby and show them your hands covered in oil/moisturiser (if using)
  • start very slow, perhaps only focusing on your baby's arms or legs the first few times
  • be very gentle and let your baby to communicate what feels good (but stop if they seem uncomfortable)
  • let your baby move to a position that allows them to be more comfortable if they want to
  • you can also do long, smooth strokes on your baby's back if they are happy to be on their tummy
  • wipe any oil off your baby's hands so they don't get it in their mouth

If you would like to learn more massage techniques for your baby, try:

  • checking your local library for a baby massage book
  • reputable resources on the internet - such as the Raising Children website
  • looking for baby massage class in your area

Torch play
If it's dark but not yet sleep time, turn off the lights for torch play! You can also try just pulling the blinds shut for torch play at any time of the day.

Why play with a torch?
Getting your baby to follow the light of a torch in a dark room helps them track their eyes along with a moving object, calms them down and is something a bit different.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • torch

How?

  • turn off the lights so the room is dark
  • turn on your torch
  • sit in a rocking chair or on the floor, with your baby on your lap
  • shine the torch at a plain wall or the ceiling
  • place your hand or fingers, or wave, in front of the torch to make shadows in the light beam - let your baby to do this as well
  • make up a few characters with your hands and tell some simple stories

What's happening with your baby at 3-4 months?
Around this age, your baby will probably start rolling from tummy to back or back to tummy - either is normal. You have probably also noticed that they now have better control of their head movements and need less support when you hold them or help them to sit up - they might even sit up by themselves with some support behind and on each side of their body!

Your baby is also busy learning about emotions and communication and probably also loves moving and grabbing at things

And, the good news is that any extra crying and fussing that might have been going on, usually settles by about 12-16 weeks too.

At 3-4 months, your baby:

  • might lift their head up high or push up on their hands during tummy time
  • is using their hands more and they might stare at them in wonder and amazement
  • is exploring the world around them by grabbing at lots of things - like rings or rattles - or putting them in their mouth
  • knows your voice and will usually turn their head towards you when you speak
  • is starting to link what you say to your facial expressions and although they love your face, new faces might also be really interesting to them too
  • is beginning to show more emotion, perhaps even laughing out loud, smiling when seeing or hearing things they like, and saying things like 'ah-goo'
  • might try talking to you in coos and sounds and when you talk, they'll listen, and perhaps try to reply - you might even hear them trying to talk when they're alone!
  • loves playing with different objects - they might also take a long look at them and shake them
  • might go cross-eyed sometimes when looking at things - this is normal in the first few months

Ice play
Letting your baby experiment through play is a great way for them to learn! They may not like playing with ice to start with, so just sit back and let them keep trying over and over on their own.

This activity can be done year round but it's particularly good on a hot day!

Why let your baby play with ice?
Introducing babies to lots of different sensations helps build up their understanding of the environment. By giving your baby opportunities to play with different textures, sounds and feelings, you're helping to build their tolerance to new experiences.

This, in turn, helps prepare them for when they start solid foods, which will present all sorts of new flavours and textures in your baby's mouth. By having a greater tolerance to new experiences, your baby should be more accepting of a range of different solids when it comes time to introduce them.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • bucket
  • large ice blocks
  • sun protection if necessary
  • large chunks of frozen fruit such as mango or pineapple rings (optional for older babies)

How?

  • dress appropriately for the weather (including sun protection if required)
  • put the ice blocks into the bucket
  • sit back and let your baby play and experience the cold and wetness of the ice
  • stop when the ice has melted into small enough pieces that your baby could choke on the bubbles
  • if your baby has started solids (usually around 6 months), try including large chunks of frozen fruit (e.g. mango or pineapple rings) in the ice bucket as well

Note: always use ice blocks (or fruit chunks) that are too big for your baby to choke on and never leave your baby alone with this activity or any other activity that involves water.

Read and tell stories
Making time to read to your baby every day is not only great for their development, but story telling is also a lovely way to bond and connect.

Why read to your baby?
Reading books helps your baby to develop their language, understand emotions and improve their eye coordination as you turn the pages.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • baby books - those with large and bright pictures are ideal
  • sun protection if necessary

How?

  • get yourself and your baby comfortable - this could be on the lounge, at the kitchen table, in a park (with sun protection if required) or just before bedtime
  • read books to your baby (and tell them other stories), getting them involved by:
    • over-exaggerating the words and changing the tone of your voice for different characters
    • pointing to different objects in the book and naming them for your baby
    • singing songs as you read

What's happening with your baby at 4-5 months?
By 4-5 months, some babies are already sitting up (especially with support around their hips and behind their bottom and lower back) and will probably be able to roll from their back to their tummy and from their tummy to their back. Others take a little while longer, so don't worry if your baby hasn't mastered these skills yet!

Giving your baby tummy time throughout each day will help build their strength and don't forget you can also help your baby practise rolling over and sitting up to further develop their strength, balance and coordination.

By now, they have probably also learned to know your voice, remember what you look like and understand that you respond when they need you - their attachment to you is growing ever stronger!

By 4-5 months old, your baby

  • has probably got to know other close family members and carers well, and understands who they are
  • really enjoys reaching and grabbing everything nearby - dangling rings, rattles, toys, small blocks and more - and using their palm and index finger, can hold things in their hand
  • might turn their head to look for something they've dropped (e.g. a spoon) although they probably won't look down for it just yet
  • will turn to you when they hear your voice, and might even respond to their name or another sound, such as a ringing bell
  • is showing more emotion:
    • blowing 'raspberries', squealing, making sounds like 'ah-goo' and even trying to copy the up-and-down tone you use when you talk
    • maybe smiling and talking at themselves (and you!) in the mirror
    • starting to get angry and frustrated and perhaps growling or whingeing instead of crying
  • may start getting interested in what you're eating and putting their fingers in their mouth - this interest will grow over the coming weeks but doesn't necessarily mean they are ready for solids yet.

Sitting up
Your baby might not yet be able to balance and stay upright, but that doesn't mean they don't have enough strength to sit up - that's what all that tummy time has been building!

Helping your baby to start sitting up will let them see their world from a different angle.

Why get your baby sitting up?
Sitting up helps your baby develop strength, balance and coordination. Your baby already has the strength in their back and neck to sit, but by helping them sit up, you're also helping them to learn how to balance and stay upright as well.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • pillows/cushions (optional)

How?

  • sit on the floor with your legs out and slightly apart
  • prop your baby in a sitting position between your legs for support (pillows/cushions can be used instead of your legs for support)
  • let your baby play while in the sitting position, e.g.:
    • with a toy on wheels to learn about 'movement'
    • sing songs with your baby where you move your baby's body while grasping their hands firmly, for example:
      • Row, row, row your boat
      • Humpty Dumpty sat on a wall
    • as your baby gets better at balancing, make the movements more involved

Try to choose songs that will help your baby anticipate movements when done over and over!

Independent play (away from screens!)
When you're busy and need to get things done, popping your baby in front of a screen can seem like the only option for some peace and quiet, even though children under the age of 2 shouldn't have any screen time at all. Using screens to keep a baby busy can also end up creating the very problem we are trying to solve by using the screens in the first place - a child who can't entertain themselves! We call this the 'screen time trap'.

This is where independent play is a much better alternative - keeping your baby entertained while encouraging their learning and development at the same time!

Why do it?
Independent play is important for your baby's development. It also decreases time in restraints such as highchairs and gives you a little bit of breathing space to get things done, without having to resort to screens for entertainment!

What you need

  • your baby
  • parent or carer (away, but close by)
  • baby safe room/space
  • rug or blanket
  • favourite toys
  • mirror (optional)

How?

  • set up a safe space, free of items that may cause harm to your baby
  • place your baby on a rug or blanket with some of their favourite toys or maybe a mirror so they can make faces at themselves
  • come and go from the room to check in on your baby

Note: always make sure you are close by and constantly checking in on your baby during independent play.

Why play peek-a-boo with food?

  • peek-a-boo must be played with another person, so it helps with their language skills and emotional and social development
  • seeing items disappear and then reappear helps babies with their thinking as they learn about what comes next and begin to understand what experts describe as 'object permanence' (knowing that an object can be there even if they can't see it)

What you need

  • tea towel

    • carrot
    • slice of orange or lemon
    • banana
    • large broccoli or cauliflower floret
    • apple

How?

  • put a small bowl/cup in front of your baby
  • place one of the food items into the small bowl/cup, making sure you point it out to your baby
  • cover the food with the tea towel
  • let your baby lift the tea towel up to discover that the food is still there

Hiding games help babies test and re-test out the idea that things don't just disappear because you can't see them (what experts call 'object permanence') while digging around for hidden objects in the sand helps babies to further develop their fine motor skills.

If they happen to lose interest before finding the hidden item, it's also a chance to encourage them to be persistent, which is another important life skill.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • sandpit
  • some colourful plastic/wooden toys
  • sun protection if necessary

How?

  • dress appropriately for the weather (including sun protection if required)
  • in a sandpit, show your baby a variety of toys
  • bury the toys
  • while over-exaggerating your expression, ask your baby "Where did that toy go?"
  • help them dig in and move the sand until they find the toy - make sure you show your excitement at a job well done!
  • over time, you can become more advanced with your hiding of items - eventually hiding items without their knowledge

Pasta play
Kids love getting messy and cooked pasta is just bursting full of options for playing and learning!!

Pasta play is easy to set up and is a great outdoor game. If your baby can stand, try doing this activity while standing at a table - this lets your baby have more freedom to move around and explore the activity fully rather than being restrained in a high chair!

Why play with pasta?
Introducing babies to lots of different sensations helps build up their understanding of the environment. Babies can practise their fine motor skills by grabbing handfuls of pasta, gripping spoons/tongs and pouring from jugs.

They can use their imaginations by pretending to cook with it and also explore its taste and texture, which should encourage them to be more accepting of it as a food.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • 1-2 packets of spaghetti (pre-cooked and cooled)
  • cooking oil (e.g. canola oil)
  • utensils (tongs, spoons etc)
  • a variety of pots, pans, bowls, jugs etc
  • bucket of water
  • outside area that is easy to clean or hose down
  • different coloured food dye (optional)
  • appropriate clothing
  • sun protection if necessary

How?

  • dress appropriately for the weather (including sun protection if required)
  • find a suitable spot outside
  • lay out the pots and pans
  • fill the bucket with some water
  • put the cooked spaghetti in a range of different bowls, with a small amount of cooking oil (to prevent clumping together
  • encourage your baby to play with and explore the pasta or just stand back and let them go - they may want to:
    • scoop and pick up the spaghetti with their hands or tongs
    • mix the spaghetti with a spoon and pretend to cook
    • taste and get used to the slippery and squidgy textures in their hands and mouth
  • add a few drops of the food dyes to different bowls/pots of pasta
    • either mix this is or encourage your baby to mix it to see what colours appear!
    • encourage your baby to fill up jugs of water and add it to the different coloured bowls to see what happens to the colour
    • stand back and let them explore!

Note: never leave your baby unattended with water - even just for a second!

Cloud dough
Babies love to mimic what you are doing. A great indoor sensory game to let your baby do this is 'cooking' with cloud dough.

Why play with cloud dough?
Introducing babies to lots of different textures and sensations helps build up their understanding of the environment. Cloud dough has just enough form to hold its shape but falls apart easily to let babies create something else!

They can practise their fine motor skills by grabbing and moulding the cloud dough and gripping cups, and use their imaginations by pretending to cook with it.

This all helps to build up their tolerance to new textures, which in turn will help with acceptance of new tastes and textures from a range of different solids foods.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your baby
  • newspaper
  • 4 cups of plain flour
  • ½ cup cooking oil (e.g. canola oil)
  • flour sifter (or sieve)
  • measuring cups

How?

  • set your baby nearby to watch you prepare
  • spread some newspaper on the floor
  • in the middle of the newspaper, pile the flour up to make a 'cake'
  • before making the cloud dough, use a cup or measuring spoons to show your baby how to scoop the flour up and empty them
  • to make the cloud dough, add the oil to the flour
  • knead with your hands until the flour has absorbed all the oil and it can be moulded into shapes
  • let your baby explore the cloud dough and make a variety of shapes together

The cloud dough can be covered and stored in the fridge for later but don't let it come into contact with water.

Note: never leave your baby alone with the flour as it can affect their breathing.

Hiding games can be great fun for your baby! This one uses one of your baby's favourite toys.

Hiding games help babies test and re-test out the idea that things don't just disappear because you can't see them, or what experts describe as 'object permanence'. It's also a chance to encourage them to be persistent, which is another important life skill.

What's happening with your baby at 12-15 months?
These few months are a busy time for your toddler!

They're busy playing and exploring, might be able to stand up and take their first steps on their own, and their language development is maturing. But it's also an important time for your toddler socially and emotionally.

Movement
Moving helps your child build muscle strength for more complex movements like standing, walking and running. If you're around while your child explores, it helps them to feel safe and builds their self-confidence.

During these months, your toddler might stand up without needing help from you or the furniture, and will probably start to walk on their own! As they get better at walking, they might start climbing stairs or even the furniture - make sure your home is ready for this stage!

If your child isn't walking on their own yet, try not to worry too much - some children won't walk without help until 15-18 months.

Behaviour, play and feelings
They're spending lots of their time working out what different things do, and what they can do with them. They'll build small towers of blocks and knock them down, scribble with a pen or crayon, and drop pegs into a basket.

After 14 months, your child might show signs of separation anxiety often and will also begin to show empathy - for example, looking sad or getting upset when they see someone else crying. This is important because learning to show empathy is essential for forming relationships with people as it's about understanding how others might be feeling.

Communication and talking
What was babbling, is starting to include real words. They might even start naming familiar objects, such as a ball.

But it's not all words just yet - they'll still grunt, nod and point to let you know what they want and might also point to people and things they know when you ask them.

At this age, your child might also:

  • hug you
  • try to help when you're dressing them, often by holding out their arms for sleeves or putting their foot up for shoes
  • drink from a cup (probably with some spills!) and use a spoon
  • point to their body parts or a favourite toy when you name them
  • follow simple instructions - for example, when you say 'Please give me the block', they should give it to you without you needing to show them what you mean
  • hold a crayon and possibly scribble with it after you show them how
  • start to pretend when playing - for example, they might pretend to drink from a cup

Get your toddler active
More active play is good for everyone! It helps us burn energy, sleep better, practise a variety of skills and stay healthy. The best (and most fun) way to achieve this is through active and energetic play!

Why get active?
From 12 months of age it is recommended that children be physically active for at least 3 hours every day. Don't worry, this isn't all at once - it should be spread throughout the day to make up at least 3 hours in total and can include all types of activity from general moving around to more vigorous activities like running.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your toddler
  • music (optional)

How?

  • try to make active play part of your family's everyday routine - a couple of ideas include:
    • walking to the park or shops instead of using the pusher or car
    • getting outside to play every day
    • dancing to music on the radio or CDs (avoid using DVDs)
    • using ride-on toys both indoors and out
    • meeting other families at the pool or park
    • collecting leaves and flowers
    • walking along the cracks (or jumping them) on the footpath
    • jump and splash in puddles when it's raining

The sun doesn't have to be shining to get active - children don't mind (and actually enjoy) being outside in the cold and rain! Just rug up, pop on a raincoat and gumboots, and watch how much fun they have splashing in puddles. Despite what our grandmothers may have told us, getting wet or being out in cold weather doesn't make us sick, it's all about wearing the right clothes!

  • a defined area to run around in (e.g. playground, backyard, lounge room)

How?

  • explain to your toddler that you will start as 'it' and they need to run away and hide so you can't catch them
  • consider including a 'base' - a safe area or spot where you/your toddler cannot be tagged by the person who is 'it'
  • as you get closer to tagging your toddler, encourage them to run away or get to 'base' so they don't get caught, emphasising the urgency and fun of not being tagged
  • when you tag your toddler, explain that it's now their turn to be 'it' and to catch you!

Tiggy can be played anywhere, with anyone at any time (probably not ideal just before bedtime though!) with any number of variations to the rules to keep it interesting for years to come!

Oobleck
Oobleck is a fun activity for everyone - it's like magic in your hands! Oobleck can flow like liquid when held loosely but is solid when held tightly in the hand. The name of the substance comes from Dr. Seuss's 1949 children's book, Bartholomew and the Oobleck. The book is about a king who is bored with the weather in his kingdom and wants something completely new to fall from the sky.

If your child can stand, try doing this activity while standing at a table (instead of sitting on a chair or the floor) - this lets them have more freedom to move around and explore the activity fully!

Why play with Oobleck?
Oobleck is a type of substance known as a 'non-Newtonian fluid' - it doesn't behave like a solid but it's not quite a liquid either! It's sure to intrigue and confuse both toddlers and adults alike, all the while helping to build up your toddler's understanding of the environment.

Toddlers can practise their fine motor skills by grabbing and moulding the Oobleck, test out how it reacts to different actions and use their imaginations in their play with it.

This all helps to build up their tolerance to new textures, which in turn will help with acceptance of new tastes and textures from a range of different solids foods.

What you need

  • parent or carer
  • your toddler
  • cornflour (sometimes called 'cornstarch')
  • water
  • bowl
  • air-tight container (for storage)

How?

  • to make Oobleck, combine equal parts of cornflour and water (e.g. cup cornflour and cup water)
  • mix well with your hands
  • keep adding more cornflour slowly until you can scoop a handful of Oobleck and almost make a ball - it should feel solid in your hands (for a second) but then quickly turns to a liquid and drips through your hands - if it doesn't, it is too thick and you'll need to add more water
  • once made, show your toddler how the Oobleck reacts if you:
    • grab handfuls and let it ooze through your fingers or squeeze it
    • stab your finger at it but then slowly let your finger sink into it
    • roll it into a ball but then stop
    • let it make a puddle then quickly drag your fingers through it
  • encourage your toddler to explore the Oobleck!

When you're finished playing with the Oobleck, either store it in an air-tight container for later or throw away in the rubbish bin (not down the sink!) to get rid of it.

Note: never leave your baby alone with the cornflour as it can affect their breathing.
