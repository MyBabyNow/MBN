4 steps to better sleep

What's the secret to a good night's sleep for the whole family? Your baby learning how to go to sleep without your help!

Use this 4 step method to deal with ongoing sleep problems in your 6+ month old:

  • find out what's causing the sleep problem
  • work on phasing this problem out over time
  • create a positive bedtime routine
  • teach your baby to settle to sleep on their own

STEPS 1 & 2: cause of the sleep problem and how to phase it out

Sleep habit How to phase out
Night feeding If your baby routinely falls asleep on breast or bottle, try feeding them before their bath or at least 20 minutes before bedtime. Feed outside their bedroom.
After 6 months, babies no longer need a night feed, so you might consider gradually phasing out the night feed.
Dummy If your baby's dummy falls out during the night and they won't go back to sleep until it's replaced, try phasing it out. You could help your baby give up the dummy or hope that as your baby gets older, they might get better at finding their dummy during the night
Music and mobiles Instead of using music and baby mobiles at bedtime, try phasing these out by turning them off before you leave the room while your baby is still awake.
Baby monitors A baby monitor may not be needed unless your baby is a long way from your room. Try not to go into your baby if they are only grizzling - give them a chance to re-settle on their own.

STEP 3: creating a positive bedtime routine

This means creating a regular series of events carried out around the same time and in the same order at each bedtime. Over time, this set of events will help your baby realise that it's time for sleep.

During the day, for example, this could start with a story, then a cuddle, then into the sleeping bag and bed. In the evening it might include a milk feed, bath time, story, cuddle and then bed.

STEP 4: teaching your baby to settle to sleep on their own

There are quite a few ways to help your baby settle to sleep on their own. A couple of popular ways include controlled comforting and camping out.

Controlled comforting

Rather than completely ignoring your babyâ€™s crying until they fall asleep (which is not recommended), controlled comforting involves quickly checking and reassuring your baby while they are still learning to settle themselves to sleep.

How to do it:

  • put your baby to bed awake
  • stay with them until they are quiet for 1 minute, then leave to allow them time to settle on their own
  • ignore any grizzling but if the crying escalates:
    • only return to provide brief reassurance or comfort
    • stay until your baby is calm (but not asleep) then leave and allow them to settle again
  • repeat these steps until your baby falls asleep on their own

Camping out

This option is good if you want to stay near to your baby but may not be the best if you easily get upset or frustrated when your baby cries. The idea is to gradually reduce the amount of help your baby needs to settle.

Over time, you might:

  • start by patting
  • then sit next to the cot
  • gradually move further away from the cot until you are outside your baby's room

Remember:

  • these are only 2 methods of teaching your baby to sleep on their own - there are other ways
  • be consistent and persistent with your approach - it sometimes takes 3-14 days to change your baby's sleep patterns. Of course, this is easier said than done when you're exhausted! Try to have a support person around and choose a good time. Alternatively, many parents find seeking professional help such as going to 'sleep school' with their babies very helpful.
  • babies are always changing and may go through times when they change their sleep habits (e.g. when they are sick, you're away on holidays etc) - you may need to repeat these methods to re-establish good sleeping habits
