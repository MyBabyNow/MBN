6+ months: Feed, play and sleep patterns

Feed, play and sleep patterns

The feed, play, sleep and repeat pattern is the basic order of a baby's day at any age.

Every baby is different, so sleep, play and feeding needs vary. As much as having a routine might be helpful, it can also be quite stressful trying to make your baby 'fit' into a specific routine!

Instead, it is best to learn your baby's hungry and tired signs and then develop a pattern that is specific to your baby and can be repeated in a similar order throughout the day.

General feed, play and sleep patterns for babies aged 6-12 months

Feed Breastfeeding: every 3-4 hours during the day and may also need 1 night feed
Formula: every 4 hours during the day and may also need 1 night feed
Play Floor play, story time and nursery rhymes, finger and toe games, push and pull toys, stacking games, visiting friends and local park, playgroup
Awake time Generally tired within 2-3 hours of waking
Tired signs Fussy with food, clingy, demanding, grizzly cry
Sleep Most have 2-3 sleeps of 1-2 hours at a time with a longer stretch of sleep at night (13-14 hours total over 24 hours)
Settling strategies Encourage self-settling

This is a guide only - feed and sleep patterns are different for every baby and are often changing. Watch for your baby's hungry and tired signs.

Feed patterns

By the time your baby has reached 6 months of age they are generally ready to start solid foods! They will still want to feed every 3-4 hours during the daytime and may still also need 1 night feed, but if your baby is developing well, this night feed can now be phased out.

Breastmilk/formula

Breastmilk or formula is still the most important source of nutrients for your baby, especially before they reach 8 months. If you're breastfeeding, continue to do so every 3-4 hours during the day but if you're using formula, offer a bottle every 4 hours or so during the day. Of course, this can vary between babies, so always remember to follow your baby's hungry signs and don't forget that your baby may still also need 1 night feed until phased out.

Introducing solids

Breastmilk or formula should always be given before solid foods until your baby is at least 8 months old. This makes sure they don't miss out on important nutrients in the formula or your breastmilk. Once your baby has reached 8 months, you could try offering solid food first, followed by a breast/formula feed.

Free play

It's important to let your baby play freely without always being held or restrained in a bouncer, pram or high chair. If your baby is on the move though, you will need to think about home safety to make sure they have got a safe space to play!

Sleep patterns

Day sleep

By 6-8 months your baby's routine is starting to change. You might find that your baby is more active during the day and now only have 2 day time sleeps of 1-2 hours each (mid morning and mid afternoon). Base your routine on your baby's tired signs.

As long as your baby is happy and content, they are probably getting enough sleep.

Night sleep

One of the biggest challenges of being a new parent is getting up to a crying baby at night and you may find yourself wondering, 'will my baby ever sleep through?'

The good news is that by 6 months, most babies will sleep longer at night, although most will still continue to wake several times a night. As they get older though, they learn to re-settle themselves.

If your baby wakes frequently at night, you might like to explore the 4 steps to better sleep section.

Managing naps while you're out and about

Not all of your baby's day naps have to be in the cot at home! It is just as important that you get out and don't feel trapped at home just so your baby can sleep!

When you're out, try to be consistent when settling your baby. For example, making sure they're always in the pram for nap time. Many (but not all!) babies sleep well in the car, so you might like to plan your day so that you are driving when it comes to nap time.

If possible, try to have a quiet day at home the next day after a busy day out - this helps your baby also learn to settle in the cot.
