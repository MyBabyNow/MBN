Wind and colic

Wind

Wind refers to the air (burping) that happens during or after a feed. Some babies take in more air than others when they feed which means they have more air to get rid of!

'Burping' can help your baby get rid of this wind, making them feel more comfortable and freeing up room in their tummy to allow for longer feeding.

Some ways to burp your baby:

  • hold your baby upright with their tummy against your shoulder and gently rub their back
  • sit your baby on your lap with one hand supporting them under their chin and the other rubbing/patting their back gently
  • lay your baby on their back for about a minute and then raise them gently to a sitting position
  • lie your baby on their tummy:
    • across your lap with one hand supporting their head and the other rubbing their back
    • along your forearm with their head near your elbow and your hand holding the nappy area

Colic

Colic is the term used to describe a baby who cries a lot (for 3+ hours a day, for 3+ days a week, over 3+ weeks) and is common in both breast and formula-fed babies. The good news is that most babies cry much less by the time they are 3-4 months of age.

We don't know what causes colic, but some common theories on why babies get colic are:

  • feeding technique- too much or too little milk, or milk given in the wrong way
  • too much wind - this may be the result of the crying as crying causes your baby to swallow wind - burping your baby can help
  • overstimulation - too much going on around your baby
  • medical causes - although it's not common, some babies may have a medical cause such as hernia, allergic response to mum's diet (in breastfed babies), reflux, infections, nappy rash or other sources of irritation

Managing colic

If your baby cries a lot, it's important to first get your baby checked by your GP or maternal and child health nurse to make sure there is nothing physically wrong - this will also help reassure you.

If there is nothing physically wrong, you might like to try:

  • different settling strategies - trial and error will tell you what works!
  • different ways of feeding - see more on breastfeeding and formula feeding

If you are worried your baby may have colic and can't seem to work out why, please talk to your GP or maternal and child health nurse.

Remember:

  • sometimes babies will cry for no reason and this is also part of normal development
  • lots of crying is usually just a phase that will pass - usually by about 3-4 months of age
  • to look after yourself - try to:
    • get enough sleep
    • take time out for yourself and
    • don't forget that you're never alone - it's OK to ask for help (or even just to talk!), so speak with family and friends or see our help and support section for plenty of other useful contacts
