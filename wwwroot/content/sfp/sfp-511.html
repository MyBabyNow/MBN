0-3 months: Feed, play and sleep patterns

Feed, play and sleep patterns

The feed, play, sleep and repeat pattern is the basic order of a baby's day at any age.

Every baby is different, so their need for sleep, feeding and playing will vary.

The key is to first learn your baby's signals for feeding, playing and sleeping, so then your baby's pattern can be repeated in a similar order throughout the day.

Can newborns follow a routine?

Most experts agree that it can be hard to get a newborn baby to follow a routine of doing the same thing at the same time each day but having some idea of what sort of feed, play and sleep pattern to expect can go some way to providing a predictable flow to your day.

That said, trying to make your baby 'fit' into a specific routine can be stressful. Every baby is different so instead, it is best to learn your baby's hungry and tired signs and then develop a pattern that best suits to your baby.

Don't forget though, that just like with us, the amount your baby feeds and sleeps can also vary from day-to-day.

General feed, play and sleep patterns for a newborn

Feed Demand feed in response to Settling strategies hungry signs, generally 8-12 feeds over 24 hours
Play Tummy time, watching faces, listening to voices, reading aloud
Awake time Usually only 1-1.5 hours
Sleep Most sleep for a 1-4 hour block (15-18 hours total over 24 hours)
Settling strategies Rhythmic gentle patting, bath, using a baby pouch, going for a walk in the pram or drive, music or singing to your baby, cuddles

This is a guide only - feed and sleep patterns are different for every baby and are often changing. Watch for your baby's hungry and tired signs.

Feed patterns

Newborns generally need anywhere from 8-12 feeds over a 24 hour period - that's why it's so exhausting!

Newborns who are being breastfed also tend to have periods each day where they feed very frequently for a few hours (cluster feeds) which are often followed by periods of longer sleeps.

Some tips for when you're first starting out:

  • Demand feed in response to your baby's hungry signs and not by the clock!
  • Offer a feed when your baby first wakes but remember to follow your baby's hungry and fullness signs.
  • Around 6-8 wet nappies a day means your baby is getting enough milk.

Play ideas

Not only is playing with your newborn fun and great for bonding, but it's also an important part of their development. All you need to get started is you and your baby!

Tummy time
Tummy time is especially important as it builds your baby's head and neck strength which is needed for lifting their head and learning to crawl, and then eventually walking!

Free play
It is important that your baby can play freely on the floor without always being held or restrained in a bouncer or pram. Just make sure you always supervise your baby and put them on their back if they fall asleep.

Sleep patterns

Your newborn will generally sleep 1-4 hours at a time, wake for a feed and a short play and then be ready to sleep again.

Newborns are generally ready for sleep again within 1-1.5 hours of waking, but this changes from baby to baby so watch out for your baby's tired signs.

What about at night?

Most parents dream of the time when their baby will sleep through the night! As tiring as it can be, it is normal for newborns to wake several times a night to feed, although this can vary a lot from baby to baby.

In general, most newborns will wake through the night to feed every:

  • 2-4 hours when aged 1-8 weeks
  • 4-6 hours when aged 8-12 weeks

To help your baby learn the difference between night and day
Keep interaction to a minimum and feed in a quiet room with dim lighting - this should help them return to sleep more quickly.
