At what age is it OK to drop the night feed?

From 6 months of age, babies who are developing well no longer need to feed overnight. It is up to you whether you choose to keep going with the night feed or drop it - either is OK. If you are unsure, discuss with your maternal and child health nurse.

Learn how to drop the night feed.

I think my baby has colic - what should I do?

Colic is the term used to describe a baby who cries a lot (for at least 3 hours a day, for 3 days a week, over a period of at least 3 weeks). It is very common and a major source of stress for new parents.

Learn more about possible causes of and ways to manage colic.

My 4 month old has started waking more at night - what can I do?

It is not uncommon for babies at 3-4 months old to start waking more at night, even if they have been good sleepers until now.

Read more about why this happens and what to do about it.

My 8 month old is still waking up several times a night - how can I encourage them to 'sleep through'?

One of the biggest challenges of being a new parent is getting up to a crying baby at night and you may find yourself wondering, 'will my baby ever sleep through?'

The good news is that by 6 months, most babies will sleep longer at night, although most will still continue to wake several times a night. As they get older though, they learn to re-settle themselves.

Find out how you can encourage your baby to sleep through the night in the 4 steps to better sleep section.

Should I feed my baby to a schedule or on demand?

Your baby knows how much and when they need to feed. Rather than pressuring your baby into a specific schedule, it is best to let your baby feed as needed (on demand) by following their hungry and fullness signs.

For a rough idea about how often your baby should feed at different ages, see feed patterns for babies aged 0-3 months, 3-6 months and 6+ months but remember, every baby is different!

Should my baby follow a set routine?

Most experts agree that it can be hard to get a baby to do the same thing as the same time each day, especially when they are under 3 months of age! Rather than a rigid schedule having a predictable flow to the day can help. At this age especially, it is best to learn your baby's hungry and tired signs and then develop a pattern that is specific to your baby.

If your baby is older than 3 months, have a look at the outlines of a typical day in the life of a baby aged 3-6 months, 6-8 months and 8-14 months, paying particular attention to the details about the typical feed, play and sleep patterns in each of these age groups.
