Introduction

Have you been thinking about giving your baby some formula in addition to your breastmilk? Or perhaps you have already started supplementing your breastfeeding with formula?

This is what's called mixed feeding – that is, your baby has both breastmilk and formula.
There are all sorts of reasons why people choose to mix feed – take our quick quiz for some suggestions specific to your situation!
Remember: before introducing formula or a bottle, try to wait until your milk supply is established (usually about 6–8 weeks after birth). This gives you and your baby a chance to figure out exactly what works best for the both of you – you making milk and your baby feeding from your breast.

If your baby doesn't seem to be growing as well as you think they should be, the first step is to talk with your maternal and child health nurse or GP. Keep in mind that babies all grow at different rates and you could be worrying for no reason!

Some possible reasons for poorer than expected growth include:
  • low milk supply which means your baby won't be getting all the energy and nutrition they need for adequate growth
  • a bad attachment which makes it harder for your baby to remove enough milk when feeding
Before jumping to conclusions though, it's important to realise that even if there is an issue, there are ways to deal with them – your baby's attachment can be checked and your milk supply can be increased. Call the ABA helpline, or ask your GP, lactation consultant or maternal and child health nurse for advice.

Remember, topping up with formula can reduce your milk supply, which means you'll need to top up with more formula, which reduces your milk supply even further (known as the top up trap). Rather than risking a reduction in your milk supply by giving your baby some formula, you could instead top up with some of your expressed breastmilk – ask your maternal and child health nurse or GP if they would support this.
'Not having enough milk' is the most common reason why mums give up breastfeeding, yet in reality, only about 5% of mums really can't make enough milk!

Take our milk supply quiz to find out if you could really have low milk supply – you might be worrying for no reason!

Keep in mind that if you do decide to supplement your baby's feeds with formula, you will likely reduce your milk supply at the same time and could potentially get caught up in the top up trap – the more you top up with formula, the more your milk supply reduces so the more formula you need to use. Instead, you might like to read about how you can make more milk.
Breastfeeding might feel a bit uncomfortable during the first week or so while you get used to it but it should never be painful!

If you're finding breastfeeding painful, your baby might not be attaching properly. Have a look at our video on getting a good attachment and make sure you ask your midwife, maternal and child health nurse or a lactation consultant to check how your baby is attaching.

You might also like to read about managing sore nipples, cracked nipples and mastitis.
Formula feeding might appear to be more convenient than breastfeeding but don't forget that there's lots of extra work involved in preparing and sterilising bottles, as well as more expensive – usually around $25 per tin!

Breastfeeding your baby, on the other hand, can be done anywhere and at any time – if breastfeeding in public worries you, check out our tips to make breastfeeding in public easier.
Have you been told that giving your baby formula will help them sleep longer at night?

Unfortunately, this often does not help. Most babies, regardless of whether they are formula-fed or breastfed, wake throughout the night for the first few months to feed. Find out more about feed and sleep patterns in newborns.

Parenting can be exhausting, so if you feel like you're always tired, it might be worth trying these tips.

You could also try:
  • sleeping in the same room as your baby so that you are close by
  • learning to breastfeed lying down so that you can keep on resting, even when feeding
If your baby is older than 6 months and they're still waking through the night, find out how you can help them (and you!) sleep through the night.
Don't be put off breastfeeding just because your returning to work!

There are many ways you can continue to breastfeed once you're working and lots of mums actually find that doing so helps them to feel connected with their baby, despite being apart more often.

Read more about options for breastfeeding when you're a working mum.
Being a new mum can be exhausting, especially when you're feeding around the clock. But, given all the benefits that breastfeeding provides, it's worth sticking with it for as long as you can!

There are plenty of things that your partner and others can do to support you that don't involve feeding, and even though your old sleep patterns may no longer be possible, there are lots of ways to make sure you still get enough sleep.

Once your breastfeeding is established (usually around 6–8 weeks), you could also try expressing so someone else can give the occasional feed. And if you are worried about your partner missing out on bonding with your baby, don't be, there are plenty of other ways for your partner to bond with your baby that don't involve feeding.
Some babies can have allergies or intolerances and react to traces of food in their mum's breastmilk, although this is uncommon and needs to be checked by a GP. If you think your baby is reacting to something in your breastmilk, speak to your GP, lactation consultant or maternal and child health nurse before making changes to your diet or switching your baby to formula.

If you can change your diet and continue to breastfeed them, instead of giving them formula, your baby will have less risk of ongoing allergies because breastfeeding is actually protective of allergies. A specialist can advise you whether this is an option for you.
If your baby doesn't seem satisfied or is fussy after breastfeeding it doesn't necessarily mean they are still hungry and needing more breastmilk!

Your milk is probably not causing your baby's dissatisfaction if you have been breastfeeding them:
  • whenever they seem hungry
  • for as long as they want to feed
  • they are attaching on well
Remember:
  • babies cry for all sorts of reasons (even after feeding), and it's not always because they are still hungry – try working through our crying baby checklist
  • for as long as they want to feed
  • if you are worried, make sure you speak with your GP, maternal and child health nurse, the ABA helpline or a lactation consultant
If you're keen to get your baby used to taking milk from a bottle, instead of formula, why not try expressing and feeding expressed breastmilk via a bottle?

This way, your breasts will still be stimulated to make milk and you'll keep up your milk supply (rather than reducing it by missing breastfeeds so you can give your baby formula in a bottle instead).

A couple of things to keep in mind:
  • avoid introducing a bottle in the first 6–8 weeks while you are still establishing breastfeeding – this gives both you and your baby a chance to figure out exactly what works best – you making milk and your baby feeding from your breast
  • once your baby is used to a bottle, it is more likely that your baby will refuse your breast (rather than the bottle) because they have to work harder to get milk from your breast!
